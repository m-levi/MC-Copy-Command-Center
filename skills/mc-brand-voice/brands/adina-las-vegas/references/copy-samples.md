# Copy Samples — Adina Las Vegas

**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-20

Annotated exemplars of ALV's strongest sends across the full playbook. When writing new copy for Adina Las Vegas, read these first. Patterns here override abstract rules when the two conflict.

Eight paragons cover the contexts ALV sends into: personal-letter launch, single-item deep-dive, sequel drop, occasion editorial (dressy + casual), teach-don't-sell, honest sale, and the pure holiday letter. When drafting, borrow the *shape* (which beats hit, how many, in what order) and the *register* (Michal's voice, mixed sentence length, specificity over superlatives) — don't imitate line-for-line.

## Sample 1 — Personal-letter launch ("Spring Into Color")

**Subject line:** New Arrivals: Spring Into Color
**Preview text:** Our new spring/summer collection is here. This season, we went full color.

**Body:**
```
NEW ARRIVALS
Spring Into Color
Our new spring/summer collection is here. And this season, we went full color.

Hey there! I'm Michal

I have butterflies sharing this one. I designed this collection the way I always imagined it — full color, solids and prints, pieces you'll actually reach for. I hope it finds its way into your life exactly the way I pictured it would.

[Featured picks: Raglan Sleeve T-shirt Dress / Valerie Midi / Michalie Midi / Mermaid Denim Maxi]

What's New This Season
01 Solids and prints that style together effortlessly
02 Now available in 1XL for most styles
03 Pieces that work together and apart

[More picks: Evie Midi / Fay Maxi / Shiri Dress / Pocket T-shirt Dress]

Color looks good on you. New arrivals are live. New sizes too.
```

**CTA:** Shop the Collection

**Why this works:**
"I have butterflies sharing this one" is peak Michal — emotional without being maudlin, vulnerable without being desperate. You can't fake that sentence. The three numbered beats do real work — "Now available in 1XL" is a concrete fit update, not "introducing inclusive sizing." "Color looks good on you" is a four-word closing line that doubles as headline and compliment — confident, warm, not "shop now before it's gone." The one exclamation point is earned because it lives in the *greeting's signature*, not the sell.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"We're SO EXCITED to share our new drop!!" — generic launch hype, zero specificity, zero first-person. The butterflies line is replaced with marketing filler. The three beats would have become "stunning silhouettes, elevated fabrics, timeless pieces."

## Sample 2 — Single-item deep-dive ("The Fay Maxi")

**Subject line:** Meet the Fay Maxi
**Preview text:** Designed to feel special, without feeling overdone.

**Body:**
```
OUR SPRING RELEASE
The Fay Maxi
Designed to feel special, without feeling overdone.

The Fay Maxi — Butter — $268
There are a few things that make this one different.

Color Story
The color is the first (and an obvious) one. I have fair skin, and I don't usually gravitate toward yellow, but this shade has warmth without washing you out. I put it on, looked in the mirror, and loved it immediately. That's how I knew it was a winner.

Fit Story
But what really makes it is the fit. There's soft ruching along the waist that creates a lifted, curved shape without feeling tight. It defines you in the right places, then opens into a full skirt that moves beautifully when you walk. The fabric is soft, fully lined, and has that perfect weight — structured enough for an event, easy enough for Shabbos or Yom Tov when you want to elevate your look. It feels feminine. Polished. Confident.

Thoughtful Details
01 Flattering waist ruching for shape
02 Full flowing skirt
03 Fully lined
04 Soft fabric with beautiful movement

Explore the Full Collection.
Find more pieces designed with the same balance in mind — feminine, flattering, and easy to wear.
```

**CTA:** Shop the Fay Maxi

**Why this works:**
"Feminine. Polished. Confident." — three sentence-fragments that literally land on the primary voice attributes. This is Michal's voice sitting on-brand without trying. "I have fair skin, and I don't usually gravitate toward yellow…" is the single most convincing move — the copywriter writing like a real person describing a real moment. "Color Story" and "Fit Story" as headers is clean editorial architecture; not "features and benefits," named sections that do real work. "Designed to feel special, without feeling overdone" is the whole Adina thesis in nine words. $268 stated once, moved on — no "worth every penny" hedging.

## Sample 3 — Sequel drop ("More Spring. More Color.")

**Subject line:** Drop 2 is here
**Preview text:** Meet the pieces that will brighten your days.

**Body:**
```
More Spring. More Color.
Meet the pieces that will brighten your days.

The response to our first drop has been unreal. And from what I've been sharing on social, I know you're going to love what's coming next.

Drop two is here. Order now for first dibs on what you love!

xoxo,
michal

[8-item product grid: image + name + price + Shop now]

Your new favorites await.
```

**CTA:** Shop New Arrivals

**Why this works:**
"The response to our first drop has been unreal" shows momentum without bragging — social proof in one sentence, no testimonial block needed. "xoxo, michal" (lowercase, casual) is a different register from the Fay Maxi's more measured close — same voice, different energy, flexed for a sequel moment. The email is short on purpose: it trusts the product grid to carry the weight, and the letter is a preamble, not the event. "Your new favorites await" — deliberately not "don't miss out." Calm confidence that the pieces will land.

**Subject line:** What I'm wearing for Seder night
**Preview text:** My picks for Pesach, pieces comfortable enough for the full Haggadah.

**Body:**
```

Shop My Picks

Shop the Evie

Shop the Marlo

Rich navy floral that reads classic without being boring. If you want something that works for Pesach, Shabbos, and every simcha after that, this is it.
Shop the Michalie

Velvet for Seder night just feels right. Fitted through the top, more relaxed below — it feels like a hug. The lightweight velvet is perfect for staying warm in the AC without overheating. And at 50% off, it's a great backup option to have in the closet.
Shop the Winter Blossom

Your Seder dress is here. Pick one. Get dressed. Enjoy the night.
```

**CTA:** Shop the Seder Edit

**Performance:** 67.91% open rate, 6.20% click rate, $17,144 in attributed revenue — the strongest paragon in the set. Use this shape for any "what to wear to X" editorial where the occasion is dressy (vort, wedding, Yom Tov seuda, any evening where the customer wants to walk in put-together and stay comfortable for hours).

**Why this works:**
"What I'm wearing, and what I think you should too" — confident, first-person, friend-of-the-customer framing. The voice of a stylish friend, not a brand. Every product description earns its place with *this customer on this night* details: "comfortable through every course," "forget you're wearing it by the end of the night," "works for Pesach, Shabbos, and every simcha after that." Mixed rhythm across products — some get three lines, some two — not templated, feels written. "Pick one. Get dressed. Enjoy the night." is a three-sentence closing doing the work of a full CTA block: imperative, short, warm, *permission to stop shopping*.

## Sample 5 — Occasion editorial, casual ("The Chol Hamoed Edit")

**Subject line:** Dressing for Chol Hamoed is tricky
**Preview text:** Look nice but not too dressy. Comfortable enough for a full day out.

**Body:**
```
TOP PICKS
The Chol Hamoed Edit
See my picks

Chol Hamoed is tricky to dress for. You want to look nice but not too dressy. Comfortable enough for a full day out, versatile enough for whatever you end up doing, but still feels like Yom Tov. I put together my favorite pieces for exactly that.

The Tops That Make It All Work
Cardigan Tee and Sleeveless Ribbed Tee are the hidden gems of this collection. They make any statement skirt truly pop because of their simplicity.
- Cardigan Tee: White
- Sleeveless Ribbed Tee: White

Perfect for spring. Pair with the Yael Top in peach or the Chevron Polo Top. Or a cardigan and boots when it's chillier.

```

**CTA:** Shop the full collection

**Why this works:**
"Tricky to dress for" names the actual problem the customer is solving — the problem *is* the hook. "I put together my favorite pieces for exactly that" is Michal's voice doing the curation work, telling the customer someone thought about this specifically. "The Tops That Make It All Work" is editorial architecture that teaches styling, not just sells pieces — this is the template for any "how to style" email. Styling callouts ("Pair with the Yael Top in peach") are specific, not "style with your favorites." Specificity is warmth.

## Sample 6 — Teach-don't-sell ("For the second days")

**Subject line:** Re-wear it. Better.
**Preview text:** The second day of Yom Tov doesn't need a new look. It needs three small moves.

**Body:**
```
For the second days
Re-Wear It. Better.
The second day of Yom Tov doesn't need a whole new look. It needs three small moves.

Tip 1 — Belt it.
A structured belt over a flowy dress changes the whole silhouette instantly.

Tip 2 — Layer with purpose.
A cardigan softens the whole look and makes a repeat outfit feel finished.

Tip 3 — New accessories, new look.
Swap in a fresh headscarf or different jewelry & re-wear a favorite guilt-free.

Save these for after Yom Tov. A few new arrivals worth coming back to.
```

**CTA:** See what's new

**Why this works:**
"Doesn't need a whole new look. It needs three small moves" reframes the customer's problem without selling them a solution. That's brand-building copy. Three tips, one sentence each — discipline. No expanded explanations. "Re-wear a favorite guilt-free" acknowledges the unspoken pressure in the customer's life (looking different every day of Yom Tov) and removes it. That's warmth at the level of lived experience. The product tie-in comes at the end and is soft — the email earned the CTA because it gave first. This is the paragon for any value-add send (newsletter slot, seasonal explainer, in-between campaign beat): lead with the idea, place product as the reward, never the lead.

## Sample 7 — Honest sale ("Winter Sale 50% off")

**Subject line:** Winter sale: your favorites, 50% off
**Preview text:** No code needed. Discount auto-applies at checkout.

**Body:**
```
Winter sale 50% off
Your Winter Favorites, Now Half the Price. No Code Needed.
Shop the winter sale

Fall Winter, On Sale

Ella Dress
Show-stopping florals meet a soft, flattering cut. Be bold.
Get Your Ella

Side Ruched Velvet Dress
Luxuriously soft velvet with strategic ruching for an effortlessly flattering silhouette.
Shop Ruched Dress

Shiri Dress
Timeless elegance in classic black. Perfect for any occasion.
Get Your Shiri

[8-item More Favorites grid]

Winter sale
Your Winter Wardrobe, Sorted. Save 50% on our entire fall/winter collection. Discount automatically applied at checkout.
```

**CTA:** Shop the winter sale

**Why this works:**
"No Code Needed" in the subhead removes friction and signals honesty — the customer knows the deal before they click. "Your Winter Wardrobe, Sorted" is a four-word mini-headline that does CTA work without saying "buy now" — solves a problem frame, not a fear frame. No urgency stacking: no "last chance," no countdown timer, no "ends tonight!!" The sale is the reason; the product is the sell. Each featured pick gets a sentence that treats it as a piece, not a markdown ("Be bold," "effortlessly flattering silhouette," "Perfect for any occasion"). "Discount automatically applied at checkout" is honest disclosure that doubles as clarity — save this exact phrasing for every auto-discount sale email.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"HUGE WINTER BLOWOUT!!! 50% OFF EVERYTHING! LAST CHANCE — ENDS TONIGHT!" Caps, countdowns, and urgency stacking — everything this voice exists to reject.

## Sample 8 — Holiday letter ("Chag Kasher V'Sameach")

**Subject line:** Chag Kasher V'Sameach
**Preview text:** *(none — the subject carries the whole moment)*

**Body:**
```
Hi Mordi,

I know how hard you've been working to make Yom Tov happen this year.

The cooking, the planning, the cleaning, the outfit coordinating (for everyone, not just yourself). Moms do it all, and I just wanted to be here for a second to say: You're doing an amazing job.

I hope this Yom Tov you get to slow down, be present with the people you love, and actually enjoy the moments you've worked so hard to create. And I hope you feel beautiful doing it.

Wishing a Chag Kasher V'Sameach to you and your family.

With love,

Michal
```

**CTA:** *(none — no product, no link, no shop button)*

**Performance:** 65.84% open rate. Zero revenue attribution by design — this send does brand work, not product work.

**Why this works:**
This is the pure personal-letter paragon — no hero image, no product grid, no CTA, no sign of the store at all. "The cooking, the planning, the cleaning, the outfit coordinating (for everyone, not just yourself)" is the line that lands — specific enough to feel *seen*, gentle enough to not perform empathy. The parenthetical aside is Michal's cadence. "Moms do it all" is a known sentiment; what makes it land is what follows — "I just wanted to be here for a second to say: You're doing an amazing job." Not a brand talking to a customer. A woman acknowledging another woman's week. The sign-off — "With love, Michal" — is the whole mode: first name, lowercase warmth, no team, no brand. Use this shape for any holiday letter, milestone letter, founder note, or moment where the brand's job is to *be with* the customer, not sell her anything. The revenue is zero and the equity gain is enormous.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Tacked a "Shop Pesach-ready pieces" CTA onto the bottom. Added a product grid "since you're dressing the family anyway." Signed from "The Adina LV Team." Every one of those moves would have ruined the moment — the email works *because* it refuses to sell.
