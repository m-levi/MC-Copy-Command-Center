# Adina Las Vegas

**Status:** canonical
**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-20

## Essence

High-end modest fashion that never makes modesty the pitch — the Aritzia of modest, written in Michal's voice.

## Audience

**Chani, 32.** Modern Orthodox, married with two or three kids, living in Teaneck, Monsey, Cedarhurst, Hollywood, or Lakewood. She shops for Shabbos, Yom Tov, simchas, and everyday. She's particular about fit — waist cinched, length right, fabric that drapes without clinging. She's the woman who messages a friend a screenshot before she checks out. She's shopped Lunella, she knows her size at By Tess, she can tell the difference between a dress that looks like she tried and one that looks like she's trying too hard. Her budget is real: she'll pay $200 for a dress that feels like $600, but she'll notice the second something cheapens. She doesn't want to look like everyone at the vort, and she doesn't want to look frumpy. She trusts Michal's taste, and she tells her friends.

**Secondary audience:** non-Jewish modest shoppers coming in through Meta ads — modest-by-preference, Muslim, observant Christian, style-preference modest. Same age range (25–45). Same emphasis on fit and quality. Less cultural context to lean on in the copy.

**List size and engagement:** engaged DTC list with strong performance on drop launches and occasion edits (Seder, Chol Hamoed, Yom Tov). Restock notifications routinely open at 60%+. Personal-letter sends from Michal open at 60%+ and drive conversation back through DMs.

Segmentation splits (voice shifts per segment):

- **Default email list (majority Jewish observant):** full cultural register — Shabbos, Yom Tov, Pesach, Chol Hamoed, simchas — assumed, not explained.
- **Meta-ads acquisition / broader-market segment:** softer cultural register — "modest" over "tznius," occasion language translated or omitted, fit and design do the work.
- **VIP / in-store shoppers:** warmer, more insider — name-drops of store events, "saw you in Hollywood last week," Michal signing personally.

## Voice

- **Is:** warm, confident, elegant, soft, refined, authentic
- **Is not:** hype-y, corporate, preachy, influencer-casual, apologetic about modesty, loud
- **Core tension:** _Luxurious but never pretentious._ High-end feel without the high-end distance — Michal's voice keeps it intimate while the product stays elevated.

**Best friend with great taste.** A real woman who designs and photographs her own pieces is writing you from her stores in Hollywood and Cleveland. She knows the fit, she knows the fabric, and she knows what to wear to a vort. She doesn't hype, she doesn't hedge, and she doesn't talk down. When she writes as herself, it's signed *— Michal.* When the brand writes, it inherits her calm, specific, occasion-aware voice without impersonating her first person.

## Writing Principles

1. **Lead with the piece, not the promo.** The first sentence should show us the Shiri Dress, the Fay Maxi, the navy restock — not the 25% off banner. Sale sends still state *why* the sale exists ("we're clearing the floor for spring"), but the piece comes first. A reviewer can point at any opener and ask: did we see a piece, a moment, or a discount percent? It should be a piece or a moment.
2. **Specific over superlative, always.** "Matte stretch crepe, cinched at the waist, side pockets you'll actually use" beats "a stunning elevated dress" every single time. If a sentence uses *stunning, gorgeous, elevated, curated,* or *luxurious* with no sensory anchor, cut it and describe the actual thing.
3. **Modesty is the frame, not the pitch.** We don't sell relief from the problem of exposure. We don't write *"finally a dress that covers!"* Our customer doesn't think of modesty as a burden, and the copy must not imply she should. Lead with fit, drape, occasion — modesty is already in the room.
4. **Michal is a real person; the brand is not a committee.** First person ("I") is reserved for Michal — holiday letters, founder notes, thank-yous, apologies. Default copy uses "we." Never mix the two in one send. Never sign *"The Adina LV Team"* — either *— Michal,* or *Adina LV,* or nothing.
5. **Shabbos, not Shabbat.** This is a house rule and it matters more than any other word choice. Exception only for a verbatim customer quote. The same discipline applies across the Jewish-calendar vocabulary — Yom Tov, Pesach, Sukkos, Chol Hamoed — use the terms; our customer knows what they mean.
6. **Soft urgency, no hard sell.** If sizes are running out, we say *"sizes going fast."* We never say *HURRY, ACT NOW, LAST CHANCE, DON'T MISS OUT.* Urgency, when used, is factual and quiet. Scarcity sells itself when the copy is calm.
7. **One CTA per email.** Two if it's a sale-plus-launch combo. Never five. Every button added after the first reduces the first one's weight.
8. **Write like it could be forwarded to her rabbi and her less-religious sister.** The twin test. If either one would wince, rewrite.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** 8–16 word sentences, mixed. One longer sentence every three or four lines for warmth. Never three long sentences back to back; never three identical-length short ones (reads as choppy/robotic). Paragraphs of 1–3 sentences. White space is part of the aesthetic.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case for subject lines, headlines, body, and CTAs. Title Case with "The" prefix for product and collection names (*The Shiri Dress, The Fay Maxi, The Spring Summer Drop, The Pesach Edit, The Armoire Collection*). All-caps banned outright (reads as shouting and instantly breaks the positioning). Rare exception: a single CTA in all-caps ("SHOP THE DROP") if the rest of the email is quiet enough to carry it.
- **Length:** subject lines under ~45 characters where possible. Promotional body 150–300 words. Editorial (The Seder Edit, The Chol Hamoed Edit) up to 400. Personal letters from Michal 150–250 words. SMS under 160 characters.
- **Emojis:** sparing. A single heart (♥︎, 💛), sparkle (✨), flower (🌸), or tulip works on seasonal sends or Michal letters. Never stack. Never in subject lines as the opener or primary hook. Emoji comfort is *low* — when in doubt, leave it out.
- **First person:** *I / my* is Michal only. Always Michal. Never used in a team-signed send (creates an ambiguous "which I?"). *We / our* is the brand-voice default and represents the team and brand. Never *"The Adina LV Team"* as a signature — clunky.
- **Punctuation quirks:** exclamation points are rare (reserve for a sell-out, a launch day; one per email maximum; never in subject lines). Em dashes fit Michal's cadence and are welcome — one or two per email. Ellipses avoided (read as teasing; use em dash or period instead). Oxford comma yes. Ampersands avoided in body copy.
- **Numbers, prices, dates:** `$128`, `$298` (full dollar sign, no `.00`). `40% off`, `25% off` (numeral + `%` + "off"). Sizes `XS–2XL` with en dashes. `April 19` in body, `April 19, 2026` if the year matters. Times always include timezone (`8 PM ET`). Free shipping threshold stated plainly: *Free shipping on $150+.*

### Michal mode vs. brand-voice mode

ALV's core writing move is knowing which of two voices to use. They are not interchangeable. Getting this wrong is the single most common way a draft reads off.

- **Michal mode** — first person, longer sentences allowed, emotional climb permitted. Signed *— Michal.* No CTA, or a very soft one. Used for: holiday letters (Chag Sameach, Chag Kasher V'Sameach), founder notes, end-of-year thank-yous, apologies for service issues, major-milestone announcements, store opening announcements.
- **Brand-voice mode** — "we/our," short and mid-length sentences, product-forward. Signed *Adina LV* or not signed at all. Used for: product launches, drops, restocks, sales, editorial/Edit sends, event invitations, most campaigns.
- **Never mix.** If the first paragraph is in Michal's voice, the whole send is. If we open with "We just restocked the Shiri," we don't close with "I can't wait for you to try it."

### No-exclamation discipline

ALV is defined as much by what we don't do as what we do. We do not sell from volume. Exclamation points, all-caps, emoji piles, and stacked urgency words are all versions of the same mistake — performing excitement instead of writing it. The rhythm of a calm sentence, with a piece named specifically, is what creates excitement in our copy.

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** under ~45 characters preferred (mobile-first).
- **Tone:** one of four modes — warm/personal, editorial/invitational, factual (restock/launch), or gently occasion-anchored. Never urgent-shouty.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case. Proper nouns and product names title-cased.

**Good (real sends, all high-performing):**

- *"Spring has arrived 🌸 Get ready to refresh your wardrobe"*
- *"Hi, Meet the Fay Maxi"*
- *"The Seder Edit ✨"*
- *"Chol Hamoed Ready"*
- *"Chag Kasher V'Sameach!"* (holiday letter from Michal — 65.84% open)
- *"Restocked: Your Favorites Are Back"*
- *"For the second days"* (teach-don't-sell)
- *"We're clearing the floor — 50% off"* (sale send with reason built in)

**Bad (do not send):**

- *"HURRY — Spring Drop is HERE!!!"* — hard-sell urgency, all-caps, exclamation stacking. Reads like any spam email in her inbox.
- *"Babe, you HAVE to see this 😍🔥"* — influencer-speak plus emoji pile. Wrong customer, wrong tone.
- *"Dear Valued Customer: New Arrivals"* — corporate-formal. The voice dies before the email opens.
- *"Finally — modest dresses that actually cover!"* — modesty-as-relief-from-a-problem framing. Our customer doesn't see herself that way.
- *"Just checking in :)"* — teaser vague-talk. Does none of the work a subject line should do.

## CTAs

CTAs are where brand voice leaks fastest. ALV's CTAs are invitational and specific — or plain-factual on a restock.

**Approved:**

- *Shop the drop*
- *Shop the edit*
- *Shop her fit*
- *See the pieces*
- *See what's new*
- *Get on the list*
- *Find my size*
- *Shop the sale*

**Banned:**

- *BUY NOW / SHOP NOW!!!* — hard-sell, all-caps. Never.
- *Don't miss out / Act now / Hurry* — manufactured urgency.
- *Grab yours / Snag one* — cheapening verbs. Wrong register.
- *Add to cart* — transactional, not invitational.
- *Tap in* — influencer-speak.

## Email Types

### Product launch / drop (campaign)

**Structure:** hero of the launch piece, 2–3 sentences of context (fabric, fit, occasion), gallery of 3–5 pieces with 1-line product descriptions, single CTA, store-locations footer.
**Copy approach:** name the piece first, the fabric second, the occasion third. Specificity does the selling. Price mentioned if the piece anchors a new price band; otherwise omitted.
**Tone shift:** mid-energy confident — "it's here, here's what it is, here's who it's for." Not manic.

### Restock (campaign)

**Structure:** opener names what's back and in which colors/sizes, 1–2 piece shots, single CTA, done.
**Copy approach:** matter-of-fact. Almost no setup. Our customer has been waiting for the Shiri in navy — deliver it without theater.
**Tone shift:** quietest register in the brand. Low-energy, product-forward. Restock sends routinely outperform launches on open rate because trust is earned, not demanded.

### Editorial Edit (Seder Edit, Chol Hamoed Edit, Simcha Edit) (campaign)

**Structure:** editorial opener teaching the wardrobe (*"Five pieces that carry you from candle lighting to the second Seder"*), moduled product blocks, occasion-specific styling notes, single CTA.
**Copy approach:** teach the wardrobe, then show the pieces. The editorial frame earns the scroll. Stakes are highest here — occasions like Pesach are emotionally loaded shopping moments.
**Tone shift:** slightly more literate. The customer is in a planning mindset.

### Sale / promo (campaign)

**Structure:** reason for the sale stated up front ("we're clearing the floor to make room for spring"), discount terms specific and honest, 2–3 piece examples, single CTA, final-sale disclosure stated in body if applicable.
**Copy approach:** we don't ratchet volume to move discount. The numbers speak. A calm send with *50% off, automatic at checkout. Final sale.* outperforms a shouty one every time.
**Overrides:** shipping line and final-sale terms move into the body, not the footer.

### Personal letter from Michal (campaign)

**Structure:** greeting (occasion or direct address), 2–4 short paragraphs in first person, sign-off *— Michal.* No product module. No CTA, or a very soft one.
**Copy approach:** warm, specific, present-tense. Real moments from Michal's life if relevant (a photoshoot, a store event, a factory trip). Never pivot to product mid-letter.
**Tone shift:** formality drops to 3/10, emotion rises to 6/10. Permission to linger.

### Holiday letter (Chag Sameach, Chag Kasher V'Sameach) (campaign)

**Structure:** one block of wish + blessing, signed *— Michal.* No product. No CTA. No shipping banner.
**Copy approach:** simple, sincere, brief. The whole point is that this email does not sell. Chag Kasher V'Sameach sent exactly this way opened at 65.84%.
**Overrides:** suspends the "one CTA per email" rule (zero CTAs here).

### Event / store (campaign)

**Structure:** date + time + address + what's happening, 1 piece or styling image, single soft CTA (*RSVP, See you there*).
**Copy approach:** event-flyer information delivered in brand voice. Still feels like Adina wrote it, not a generic flyer.

### Welcome (flow)

**Structure:** greeting, brief brand story (2 sentences), fit philosophy (1 sentence), one piece spotlight, discount code if applicable, sign-off from Michal.
**Copy approach:** introduce the brand through the fit promise. Don't over-explain modesty. Assume the subscriber is already partway in.
**Tone shift:** warmer, slightly more first-person. Michal may sign this one.

### Abandoned cart (flow)

**Structure:** "your size might go" opener, 1 image of the cart piece, sizing or fit note, soft CTA to resume, shipping threshold reminder.
**Copy approach:** assumes good faith. "Your size might go" is honest without being pushy. No discount in email 1.
**Tone shift:** matter-of-fact, not desperate. Closer to a quiet nudge.

### Post-purchase (flow)

**Structure:** thank-you in Michal's voice, care/wash note if applicable, invitation to share a fit review or tag the brand.
**Copy approach:** gratitude tone, no selling. The customer just bought — the brand's job now is to make her happy she did.

### Win-back (flow)

**Structure:** light acknowledgment of absence ("it's been a minute"), what's new since she last shopped (one or two pieces or one recent drop), soft CTA.
**Copy approach:** candid, slightly self-aware. Never guilt-trippy. Never *"we miss you."*
**Tone shift:** slightly more personal, borrowing a little from Michal mode.

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **Calling modesty a limitation.** No *cover up, hide, modesty rules, finally a dress that actually covers.* This reframes modesty as a burden — which is not how our customer thinks, and not how the brand speaks.
- **"Tznius" as the default word for modest.** Acceptable in explicit in-group moments (community event captions, pop-up posts). *"Modest"* is the default.
- **"ALV" in customer-facing copy.** *Adina Las Vegas* (formal first mention), *Adina LV* (casual, SMS, footer, second reference). Never *ALV.*
- **"The Adina Las Vegas Team" as a signature.** Clunky and cold. Either *— Michal,* or *Adina LV,* or no signature.
- **First-person "I" in a brand-voice send.** Creates a "which I?" problem. First person belongs to Michal mode only.
- **Negative competitor comparisons.** We don't name Lunella, By Tess, Udel, Avanel Couture, or Shein-adjacent brands in copy, even by implication. Our differentiator is fit, fabric, and Michal — we don't need to say what's wrong with anyone else's.
- **Filler-luxury words as default adjectives.** *Stunning, gorgeous, iconic, elevated, curated, luxurious, handcrafted, mindful, intentional.* Technically correct for our positioning, but drained of meaning by overuse. Use once per quarter, with intention, or not at all. *Elevated* especially — once a quarter at most.
- **Emoji stacking in subject lines or body.** One emoji per email ceiling, and only when it earns its place (seasonal, holiday, personal letter).
- **Discount-leading subject lines and headers.** The piece comes first, the discount supports. *"Spring pieces are live — 25% off through Sunday"* works; *"25% OFF SITE-WIDE!!!"* does not.
- **Frumpy / boxy / shapeless as counter-framing.** Even to position what we're *not,* don't name what others are by insulting them. Builds the brand by dragging someone else down — wrong posture.

_Universal banned moves apply automatically — see `mc-brand-voice/references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

## Design References

- **Voice references:**
  - *Aritzia* — design-forward, feminine, trend-aware but not trend-chasing. The tonal target when the brand writes about its own pieces.
  - *By Tess* — borrow the *clarity of POV,* not the volume or trend cycle. Punchy-girly with matcha/zodiac energy is not us; the unembarrassed confidence is.
  - *Shop Dolly* — borrow the *confidence.* They sell with confident over-promising; we sell with confident specificity. Same confidence, different technique.

- **Visual references:**
  - *By Tess* (primary) — block-built emails, consistent fonts, mix of moving and still images, engaging-but-not-overstimulating. Per Michal's questionnaire: *"their branding and fonts are consistent, they have a good mix of moving images and still images to keep things engaging but not overstimulating."*
  - *Aritzia* — clean product photography, editorial rhythm, generous white space.
  - *Reformation* — feminine silhouettes, considered palettes, restrained typography (not the tone — the visual calm).

- **Avoid visually:** cluttered sends, harsh discount banners, too many fonts, stacked graphics, bright/loud color blocks, anything that reads as cheap or busy.

## Extras

**The name.** Adina is built from Michal's three oldest kids — **A**vi, **Di**na, **Na**omi. Ellie (Eliana Chana) joined the family in 2022. Las Vegas is where Michal lived when she founded the brand; she now lives in Miami. The name is personal, not aspirational.

**The founding story.** Michal started Adina Las Vegas in 2020 as a young mom of three who couldn't find modest clothes that felt current, flattering, or fun. Early production was in Mexico (her Venezuelan Spanish helped), which capped quality; by the time the brand scaled she moved production to China, partnering with the same caliber of factories that supply luxury modest brands. The brand's value proposition — premium quality at an accessible price — comes out of that manufacturing arc.

**When to use the origin story in copy.** Not in every email. It's a reservoir for the About page, press, founder Q&As, anniversary sends, store-opening announcements. Not a recurring hook.

**Scale-mode candor.** Michal is honest about the tradeoffs of growth — sizing consistency, collection cohesion, communicating unique value at scale. That same honesty permissions ALV's copy to be candid where competitors hedge. *"Runs snug through the waist — size up if you're between"* is on-brand precisely because Michal talks about the brand this way.

**The twin test.** Every send should pass two readers: the customer's less-religious sister (who shouldn't be alienated by in-group language without context) and her rabbi (who shouldn't find anything that embarrasses the community). If either one would wince, rewrite.

**Permissive exception.** Exclamation points are rare, but allowed once per email on a genuine sell-out, a launch day, or a Michal personal letter — never stacked, never in subject lines. If the draft has more than one, cut to one.
