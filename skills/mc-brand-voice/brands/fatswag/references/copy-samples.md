# Copy Samples — FatSwag

**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-19

Annotated exemplars of FatSwag's strongest sends. When writing new copy for FatSwag, read these first. Patterns here override abstract rules when the two conflict.

A broader archive of sends (including A/B subject pairs and full side-by-side right/wrong comparisons) lives in the legacy `fatswag-brand-voice` skill's copy-samples file. This reference holds the canonical set — the shortest path to calibrating voice.

---

## Sample 1 — Weekly Drop ("You already know")

**Subject line:** You already know.
**Preview text:** New drop just went live.

**Body:**
```
NEW HEAT JUST DROPPED.
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE DROP

**Why this works:**
Every move here is restraint. The subject assumes the reader is already locked in — no explanation, no hook, just the signal. The body is a single all-caps fragment that labels what's new without describing it. "NEW HEAT JUST DROPPED" is literal and cultural at the same time. The design does everything else.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Described the collection, listed feature bullets, added "we're so excited to share" — turned the drop into a pitch.

## Sample 2 — Weekly Drop ("FOR THE PEOPLE")

**Subject line:** New drop. Friday.
**Preview text:** 4 new pieces on the site.

**Body:**
```
FOR THE PEOPLE.

This week's drop is live.
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE DROP

**Why this works:**
"FOR THE PEOPLE" is the community-first principle in three words. The sub-line ("This week's drop is live.") is a fact, not a pitch — it announces, doesn't sell. Subject + preview together give the reader the full facts (new drop, when, how many pieces) before they even open. The writer added nothing; nothing needed adding.

## Sample 3 — Weekly Drop (conversational opener)

**Subject line:** Wyd?? New drop just hit.
**Preview text:** Premade. Ships tomorrow.

**Body:**
```
HVY FIT.

Premade. Ready to ship.
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE DROP

**Why this works:**
The "Wyd??" opener lifts straight from Swag's SMS voice — it reads like a real text, not a marketing line. "HVY FIT" as a headline is vocabulary check-passing: it's what Swag actually calls a good piece. The two-line product feature ("Premade. Ready to ship.") does the work a paragraph would do elsewhere — material, shipping, done.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"Hey there! We wanted to let you know about our newest heavyweight drop, designed with premium materials..." — fake friendliness, marketing cadence, zero DNA.

## Sample 4 — Beanie Drop

**Subject line:** Wake up. New beanies.
**Preview text:** 4 new colors. Pre-order.

**Body:**
```
THE CROWN IS BACK.

4 new colors. Pre-order only.
These will sell out.
```

**CTA:** COP YOURS

**Why this works:**
Beanie emails lean harder into scarcity and speed than clothing drops — this one stacks three urgency signals (pre-order only, "will sell out," COP YOURS) without any of the fake-urgency language ("HURRY!!" "LAST CHANCE!!!") the universal banned list kills on sight. "THE CROWN" is Swag's own word for the beanie. "Wake up" is a confirmed opener from the SMS vocabulary. Every line earns its place.

## Sample 5 — Sale / Promo (flash)

**Subject line:** 1 hour only.
**Preview text:** 25% off. Right now.

**Body:**
```
FLASH SALE.

25% off. Next 60 minutes.
Tap in or miss out.
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE SALE

**Why this works:**
The window is the story — not the discount. "Next 60 minutes" is a real fact, not a manufactured urgency — when the email says 60 minutes, the sale is actually 60 minutes. "Tap in or miss out" reads like a text from Swag, not a copywriter trying to sound streetwear. Six lines of copy including the CTA. That's all it takes.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"Don't Miss Our Limited-Time Flash Sale! For the next hour only, enjoy an incredible 25% off our entire collection..." — bloated, breathless, corporate.

## Sample 6 — VIP / Exclusive Access

**Subject line:** Don't share this.
**Preview text:** Before IG. Before SMS.

**Body:**
```
EARLY ACCESS.

You're seeing this before IG.
Before SMS.
Before everybody.
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE DROP

**Why this works:**
"Don't share this" is an insider frame — the subject makes the reader feel picked, not pitched. The body uses parallel fragments ("Before IG. Before SMS. Before everybody.") to land the exclusivity without ever using the word "exclusive." The voice is quieter here than a drop email — this is the VIP tone shift. Same vocabulary, same restraint, lower volume.

## Sample 7 — Product Feature (sweat set)

**Subject line:** Heavy fit. Ready to ship.
**Preview text:** Soft. Heavyweight. All day.

**Body:**
```
THE NEW SET.

Heavyweight. Soft. All day.
Premade. Ships tomorrow.
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE DROP

**Why this works:**
Classic FatSwag product feature: spec-sheet fragments with cultural energy. Four callouts total ("Heavyweight. Soft. All day. Premade. Ships tomorrow.") and every one answers something a customer actually cares about — weight, feel, wearability, availability, speed. No adjectives, no "premium quality," no "designed with comfort in mind."

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"This premium heavyweight fleece features a relaxed fit designed for all-day comfort, crafted from soft cotton-blend material..." — the anti-version the principle exists to prevent.

## Sample 8 — Abandoned Cart, Email 2 (discount stage)

**Subject line:** Wyd?? Come finish this.
**Preview text:** 10% off to close it out.

**Body:**
```
IT'S STILL HERE.

10% off to close it out. Code: COMEBACK
```

**CTA:** COMPLETE YOUR ORDER

**Why this works:**
Same voice as a drop email, slightly warmer tone for the flow context — Swag pulling you back in, not selling you something new. "Come finish this" lands as a nudge, not a pitch. The discount gets one line. The CTA language ("COMPLETE YOUR ORDER") is purpose-specific, not generic. Email 1 in the flow carried no discount — this one earns it by being the second touch.

## Sample 9 — Welcome Flow, Email 1

**Subject line:** Welcome to FatSwag.
**Preview text:** New drops every week.

**Body:**
```
COME GET FLY.

New drops every week. You hear it here first.

— Swag
```

**CTA:** SHOP THE DROP

**Why this works:**
Welcome is the one place Swag's first-person is on. "Come get fly" is _his_ phrase (from the origin quote) — using it here ties the whole brand back to the person writing the email. The sign-off (`— Swag`) seals it. Still no paragraphs, still no brand explanation ("we're a size-inclusive streetwear brand that believes..."). The flow gives the reader what they need: who this is, how often drops come, and that they heard first.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"Welcome to the FatSwag Family! We're So Glad You're Here! Thank you so much for joining our community..." — the anti-version lives in the old side-by-side archive. Everything that can go wrong in a welcome email goes wrong there.

## Sample 10 — Post-Purchase (shipping)

**Subject line:** Your order shipped.
**Preview text:** Track it now.

**Body:**
```
ON THE WAY.

Your FatSwag order just shipped.
```

**CTA:** TRACK YOUR ORDER

**Why this works:**
Post-purchase is gratitude and community, not another pitch. Three lines total. "ON THE WAY" as the headline does the job a whole paragraph would do in a corporate email. No upsell, no cross-sell, no "while you're waiting, check out..." The customer already bought. Let them have the moment.
