# Kosher Casual — Pre-Send Checklist

Run this before any Kosher Casual send goes out. The two most common failure modes for this brand are **choppy-fragment catalog voice** and **luxury drift** — if nothing else gets caught, catch those.

For deeper voice rationale, see `brand.md` and `copy-samples.md`. This file is the fast pre-handoff scan.

---

## The fragment test (the #1 failure mode)

Read the email out loud. Does it sound like speech, or like an Instagram caption?

1. **Full-sentence discipline.** *"New shell tops. Soft fabric. Stays put."* is off-brand even if every word is approved. *"Our shell tops stay in place all day, made with soft stretch fabric that won't ride up or lose its shape."* is on-brand. Short paragraphs are fine. Choppy fragments are not.
2. **No three short clipped sentences in a row.** Mix sentence length. If three consecutive sentences are under 8 words, rewrite the middle one.
3. **No bullet lists that replace prose.** Bullets are fine for genuine lists (three care tips, four sizes). They are not a shortcut for avoiding full sentences in the opener.

## Vocabulary (the non-negotiables)

4. **No luxury / premium drift.** Search the draft for: *luxury, premium, curated, elevated, boutique, essentials (as descriptor), capsule, high-end, sophisticated, refined.* Any one of these reads as the wrong brand. We are the Old Navy of modest — not Nordstrom.
5. **No fashion-trend language.** Search for: *on-trend, fashion-forward, trendsetter, it-girl, this season's must-have, the [X] of the season, statement piece.* Our customer is here because she doesn't want trends. Saying "trend" contradicts the core promise.
6. **No modesty-as-preaching framing.** Search for: *modesty rules, cover up, hide, conceal, tznius (on broadcast), modesty standards, keep yourself covered.* Modesty is the frame, not the pitch. She signed up because we solve coverage — not because she needs a lecture.
7. **No modesty-as-relief framing.** Search for: *finally a dress that covers, no more awkward outfits, at last — something modest.* Same failure mode as the preaching check, from the opposite direction. Her coverage need is assumed, not narrated.
8. **No body-image or appearance language.** Search for: *flattering, slimming, tummy control, hides, smooths, lengthens, forgiving (as body descriptor), figure-flattering.* We sell clothes that fit well and work. Never clothes that "fix" the body.
9. **No influencer-speak.** Search for: *babe, bestie, queen, girlie, hun, sis, obsessed (as our exclamation).* Wrong customer, wrong voice.
10. **No hype-urgency.** Search for: *HURRY, LAST CHANCE, DON'T MISS OUT, ACT NOW, SELLING FAST, GONE, run don't walk, final hours.* Universal ban, reiterated here because every sale draft reaches for one.
11. **No over-technical fabric copy.** Search for: *shape retention technology, moisture-wicking performance knit, four-way mechanical stretch, engineered fabric.* If a sentence would fit on a Dick's Sporting Goods product page, rewrite it in plain language. *"Soft stretch Turkish fabric that holds its shape"* does the same job.
12. **No corporate preamble.** Search for: *Introducing our new [X], We are pleased to announce, We hope this email finds you well, Dear valued customer.* Zero tolerance.

## Faith & audience register

13. **Faith-agnostic on broadcast.** Default to *modest, layering, coverage.* Use *Shabbos, Yom Tov, Pesach, Chol Hamoed, tznius* only on explicitly segmented Jewish-audience sends — never on default list.
14. **Christian/multi-faith subscriber read-through.** Imagine an Apostolic Pentecostal subscriber in Tennessee reading this send. Does anything feel in-group-excluding? If yes, rewrite or segment.
15. **"Modest" is the default category word.** Not *modest fashion* (adds the wrong frame), not *tznius* (wrong segment), not *covered* (adjective, not category).
16. **New subscribers / TikTok Shop.** If the list is acquisition-heavy, assume less context. Explain what shell tops are and why consistency matters.

## Openers

17. **Benefit in the first two sentences.** If a subscriber only reads the subject line and the first two lines, would she know what this email is about *and* what's in it for her? If she'd only know a product name exists, the opener failed.
18. **Lead with the piece's job, not its name.** *"Our shell tops stay in place from morning carpool to evening errands"* beats *"The shell top is back."*
19. **No promo-banner opener.** *"20% off shell tops — this week only"* is not the first sentence. The benefit reminder is; the offer lives in sentence two or three.
20. **Exception — operational-update sends.** When the news is the warehouse move, stockout resolution, or denim return, the news itself is the opener. Benefit-first rule suspends for these sends only.

## Structure & format

21. **Subject line 40–60 characters (6–10 words).** Mobile-first. Sentence case. Never emoji. Never all-caps. Never exclamation point in a subject line.
22. **Preview text extends the subject, doesn't repeat it.** 40–100 characters.
23. **Sentences 10–18 words, mixed.** One 25-word sentence is fine; a paragraph of them is not. A paragraph of 5-word sentences is not fine either — reads as choppy.
24. **Paragraphs 1–3 sentences.** Short but complete. White space is welcome; sentence fragmentation is not.
25. **Sentence case everywhere.** Subject lines, headlines, body, CTAs. No Title Case headlines.
26. **No all-caps for emphasis.** Ever, in marketing copy.
27. **No emoji.** Not in subject lines, not in body, not in CTAs, not in SMS. Hard line. Every sale draft wants to reach for a fire emoji. Don't.
28. **One em dash or two per email, maximum.** Em dashes are welcome for natural emphasis — but not in every sentence.
29. **Exclamation points — at most one per email, never in subject lines.**
30. **Oxford comma — yes, always.**
31. **Contractions used naturally.** *We've, you're, that's.* Writing them out (*we have*) reads as starched.
32. **Numbers as numerals.** *20+ years, 3 colors, 15% off.*

## CTAs

33. **Name what's being shopped.** *"Shop shell tops"* / *"Shop the basics"* / *"Find your fit."* Never bare *"Shop Now"* — too generic.
34. **Banned CTAs.** *Elevate your style, Discover, Unlock, Grab yours, Snag one, Don't miss out, Last chance, Hurry.*
35. **One CTA per email.** Two if it's a genuinely paired campaign (product + restock). Never three.

## Sign-off

36. **Default:** *The Kosher Casual Team,* or no signature at all.
37. **Never:** *XOXO, Warmly, Cheers, Stay stylish, Yours truly, Kindest regards.*
38. **No single-name founder signature** on broadcast product emails — no "Mordi mode" equivalent. If a future personal note goes out, it's flagged as that, not a surprise.

## Offer framing

39. **Frame sales as "stock up," not "don't miss out."** *"Stock up on shell tops — 20% off through Thursday"* is on-brand. *"FINAL HOURS: 50% OFF"* is not.
40. **Deadline stated factually.** *"Sale ends Thursday at midnight."* No countdown theater, no "final hours" language.
41. **Reason-for-sale, when real, is named.** *"We're clearing seasonal colors for the spring drop"* beats a mystery sale. Honesty is brand-positioning.
42. **Discount range check.** Campaigns typically 15–30%. Welcome/winback a small offer at most. If a draft uses 50%+, flag to confirm.

## Honesty & operational transparency

43. **Warehouse / shipping references.** When a draft mentions shipping, check that it reflects current state (US warehousing primary; some older stock still ships from Israel). Don't claim faster shipping for orders we know will ship from Israel.
44. **Denim / stockout language.** Denim is back after years out of stock. Reuse "still fits the way you remember" — don't pitch it as new-and-improved.
45. **Operational-rumor sends.** If a draft is addressing something a customer has heard through the grapevine (warehouse move, shipping delay, stockout), state the specifics plainly. Vague positivity is a tell.

## Quick Kill List

If any of these appear, the email isn't ready:

- Any emoji anywhere (subject, body, CTA, sign-off, SMS)
- Any all-caps word for emphasis
- Any exclamation point in a subject line
- Any *luxury, premium, curated, elevated, boutique, capsule, essentials*
- Any *on-trend, fashion-forward, trendsetter, must-have*
- Any *flattering, slimming, tummy control, figure-flattering, hides, smooths*
- Any *modesty rules, cover up, hide, conceal*
- Any *finally — a dress that covers* framing
- Any *babe, bestie, queen, girlie, obsessed (in our voice)*
- Any *HURRY, LAST CHANCE, DON'T MISS OUT, final hours, selling fast*
- Any *Introducing, We are pleased to announce, Dear valued customer*
- Any three-clipped-fragment paragraph (the catalog-voice tell)
- Any bare *Shop Now* / *Buy Now* CTA without specifying the category
- Any *tznius, Shabbos, Yom Tov* on a broadcast (non-segmented) send
- Any *XOXO, Warmly, Cheers, Stay stylish* sign-off
- Any over-technical fabric jargon (*shape retention technology* etc.)

## Tone-Shift Safety Checks

Voice stays constant. Tone shifts per email type. Confirm the send matches its assigned tone:

- **Restock:** matter-of-fact. The customer was waiting — don't over-sell.
- **Product education:** slightly warmer. She's in a learning moment, not a buying moment. The email should be worth opening even if she doesn't click.
- **Sale / promo:** calm, specific. Frame as stock-up, not urgency. The discount supports the email; it doesn't carry it.
- **Welcome flow:** warmer, orienting. Say what we do in the first 30 words. Honest about why we're reliable.
- **Cart abandonment:** empathetic, service-first. Offers help, not pressure. No discount in email 1.
- **Post-purchase:** gratitude only. No cross-sell. Make her glad she bought.
- **Win-back:** honest and slightly self-aware. "Here's what's back" beats "we miss you." Good place to surface operational wins (faster shipping, denim return).
- **Transparency / operational:** factual opener. Name the change. Thank customers for patience if warranted. Suspends the benefit-first rule — the news is the benefit of opening.

## Faith-audience final check

Before hitting send on a broadcast, read the email twice:

1. **As the Modern Orthodox subscriber in Teaneck:** Does *"modest"* as the category word feel natural, or does the absence of *Shabbos / Yom Tov* feel thin? On broadcast, it should feel natural — cultural shorthand belongs in segmented sends.
2. **As the Apostolic Pentecostal subscriber in Tennessee:** Does anything in the copy feel like it assumes Jewish-community context? If she encounters *tznius, Chol Hamoed,* or unexplained Jewish-calendar references on a broadcast, segmentation failed.

If either audience falls out, either rewrite the copy faith-agnostically or split into two segmented sends.

---

## Reviewer handoff

Once this checklist passes, the send is ready for approval. Log any deliberate exceptions (Jewish-audience-segmented send using full cultural register, founder-voice note, operational-update send suspending benefit-first rule) so the deviation is traceable, not accidental.
