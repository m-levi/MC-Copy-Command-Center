# The Really Good Whisky Company

**Source:** migrated from `really-good-whisky-brand-voice/SKILL.md` (v1)
**Status:** canonical
**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-20

## Essence

London-based curator of exceptional whisky — 1,200+ hand-picked bottles, worldwide shipping, built around the Really Good Club membership community. Founded 2019. The voice is **Apple's balance of luxury and accessibility, with a drop of British wit.** Premium without pretension. Expert without gatekeeping. Elegant without stuffy.

## Audience

30–65+ (core 40–55), affluent (£60K–£250K+ / $80K–$300K+), university-educated, predominantly male (70–80%) with a growing female segment. Global — concentrated in UK, US, Europe, Japan, Asia-Pacific.

They identify as **connoisseurs**, not shoppers. They say _"I'm looking for something special,"_ not _"I need whisky."_ They want to discover and explore. They value authenticity, heritage, craftsmanship, and curation. They want to be recognized as people with taste — and they want to be treated accordingly.

Two postures the copy needs to honour:

- **Enthusiasts** want to feel known — specific age statements, cask finishes, distillery stories, nuanced tasting notes.
- **Curious newcomers** want to feel welcomed — vocabulary explained in passing, guidance offered, no "you should already know this" energy.

A single email should leave both segments feeling respected.

## Voice

- **Is:** premium, refined, approachable, warm, expert, elegantly witty
- **Is not:** stuffy, pretentious, corporate, academic, aggressive, cheesy, casual
- **Core tension:** _Luxurious but accessible._ The voice must feel high-end without creating distance — like walking into a beautifully designed boutique where the staff is genuinely happy you came in.

## Writing Principles

1. **Premium without pretension.** Elevated vocabulary — "exceptional," "curated," "hand-picked," "craftsmanship" — used naturally, not stacked into parody. One "rather special" per email lands; three feels like costume.
2. **Expert, worn lightly.** Use precise whisky terminology ("cask strength," "sherry finish," "age statement") and explain it in passing where it might not be known. Expertise serves the reader; it doesn't audition for them.
3. **Specific over generic.** "Aged 18 years in sherry casks, with notes of dark chocolate and a whisper of smoke" beats "exquisite whisky" every time. Sensory detail does the selling.
4. **Discovery, not desperation.** Sales read as opportunities to discover — not as clearance events. Urgency is real ("only 30 bottles," "this weekend") or it doesn't appear.
5. **One drop of wit per email, maximum.** British understatement, clever turn of phrase, occasional personification ("your cabinet has been waiting"). Never a pun fest. Never self-deprecating. If the line doesn't come naturally, cut it — forced wit is worse than no wit.
6. **Write like a letter from a knowledgeable friend.** Contractions, natural rhythm, get to the point without preamble. Corporate-speak ("we cordially invite you to peruse") is as wrong here as slang ("grab these sick whiskies") — both break trust with the reader.
7. **Community, not transaction.** The Really Good Club is a community of connoisseurs — not a loyalty program. Membership language positions first access, insider benefits, belonging. Members save; non-members wait a day.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** short to medium sentences, 8–15 words ideal, 20-word ceiling for most. Active voice. Occasional fragments for emphasis ("Hand-picked. Expertly chosen.").
- **Capitalization:** sentence case throughout — subject lines, headlines, body, CTAs. Only brand names and proper nouns capitalize. All caps is banned outright (it reads as shouting and breaks the premium positioning instantly).
- **Length:** subject lines 35–55 characters. Promotional body copy 150–300 words. Educational up to 400. Paragraphs capped at three sentences.
- **Emojis:** never. In subject lines, body, or CTAs. The brand is too refined — an emoji reads as a tonal crack.
- **First person:** "we" is the brand. Sign-off is always "Cheers, The Really Good Whisky Company Team" — never an individual person. This preserves brand voice consistency.

### Punctuation & Signature Moves

RGW has a handful of typographic moves that function as identity markers. Consistency here is what makes a send unmistakably RGW before the reader processes the words.

- **Em dashes — used for elegant breaks in thought.** Not hyphens. Not two dashes. One em dash per email on average; two is the ceiling. Example: _"This isn't your everyday dram — aged 18 years in sherry casks."_
- **Arrows (→) for inline lists.** When a list appears in an educational or promotional email, use `→` instead of traditional bullets. This is a visual signature that ties the brand to a clean, modern, design-forward aesthetic. Reserve bullets (•) for technical spec callouts only.
- **Oxford comma, always.** "Rare, limited, and exclusive." Non-negotiable — it's part of the voice's precision.
- **British spelling throughout.** flavour, colour, favourite, honour, curated. A single Americanism breaks the quintessentially-British positioning.
- **Contractions naturally.** "We've," "you're," "it's" — maintains conversational warmth. Writing them out ("we have," "you are") reads as starched.
- **Exclamation marks — rarely.** At most one per email, and only in genuinely informal contexts. The period is the default punctuation of the voice; full stops carry more weight than exclamation.
- **Sentence fragments, sparingly.** "Hand-picked. Expertly chosen. Delivered with care." Three fragments in a row is the move — more than three and it reads as try-hard.
- **"Whisky" is always singular.** _Never_ "whiskies." This is the single most important vocabulary rule in the brand. A reviewer who catches "whiskies" anywhere in a draft sends it back.

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** 35–55 characters. Mobile-first.
- **Tone:** benefit-driven with subtle intrigue. Lead with what's most compelling — rarity, specificity, member benefit, or seasonal hook. Avoid clickbait and avoid hype.
- **Emoji:** never.
- **Good:**
  - "Your next extraordinary dram has arrived"
  - "Limited edition: Only 47 bottles left worldwide"
  - "Members save 20% on this week's rare finds"
  - "The Islay collection your cabinet's been waiting for"
  - "Behind the barrel: How we choose exceptional whisky"
  - "Spring's finest single malts are here"
  - "Last call: This Highland gem sells out tomorrow"
  - "Your cabinet has been waiting" (re-engagement)
- **Bad:**
  - "OMG! HUGE SALE! 50% OFF EVERYTHING!!!" — all caps, hype, breaks premium positioning.
  - "Check out these sick whiskies" — casual, uses forbidden plural.
  - "Don't miss out on this once-in-a-lifetime opportunity" — clichéd, manufactured urgency.
  - "Quarterly Newsletter: Product Updates" — corporate, no pull.

## CTAs

- **Style:** invitational, benefit-focused, 2–4 words, title case or sentence case, never all caps.
- **Approved vocabulary:**
  - Explore the Collection
  - Shop Rare Releases
  - Claim Your Discount
  - Discover Your Dram
  - View the Bottle
  - Join the Really Good Club
  - Complete Your Purchase
  - Explore What's New
- **Banned generics:**
  - BUY NOW / SHOP NOW — too aggressive, too generic, wrong register.
  - Click Here — no benefit, no context.
  - Shop Whiskies — forbidden plural.
  - Don't Miss Out — desperate.
  - Add to Cart — transactional.

## Email Types

**Voice stays constant. Tone shifts with context.** A limited-release announcement leans into discovery and scarcity; a welcome leans into gratitude and orientation; cart abandonment leans into service and reassurance. None of them break the premium register — what changes is the emotional posture the voice holds for that moment.

### Limited Release / New Arrival (primary campaign)
- **Structure:** hook (1–2 sentences of intrigue) → specifics (age statement, cask, tasting notes, provenance) → scarcity or member access → CTA → warm close.
- **Copy approach:** lead with "something rather special"–style understatement. Use specific numbers ("only 200 bottles made worldwide, we've secured 30"). Sensory detail carries the selling — no need to tell the reader it's premium when the description proves it.
- **Tone shift:** heightened — this is the moment the voice delivers its most elegant, most curated version. Restraint with vocabulary; extravagance with specificity.

### Educational / Value-Add
- **Structure:** relatable opening question → promise of value → 3–5 definitions or insights (arrows `→` work well here) → gentle product tie-in → discovery CTA.
- **Copy approach:** 80% education, 20% product. Breaks complexity into accessible insights without dumbing them down. Uses "your" to personalize ("Here's your quick guide to…").
- **Tone shift:** warmer and more conversational than a campaign send. The voice is the friend who knows everything about whisky and never makes you feel ignorant.
- **Overrides:** CTA can be softer and later in the email than a promotional send — the value-add does the goodwill work, not the button.

### Membership / Really Good Club Recruitment
- **Structure:** aspirational hook → value proposition → benefits list (always arrows `→`, 4–5 items) → ROI framing → community framing → "Join the Really Good Club" CTA.
- **Copy approach:** position membership as a smart investment in an exceptional collection — not a loyalty program. "Members get first access" beats "sign up for our newsletter."
- **Tone shift:** more aspirational than discovery; more insider than broadcast. "The insider access your collection deserves" is the register.

### Sale / Seasonal Promotion
- **Structure:** seasonal or thematic hook → offer stated clearly → member-first access language → supporting benefit (shipping, code) → CTA → support offer.
- **Copy approach:** premium positioning is maintained throughout. "Spring has arrived, and so has something rather lovely: 20% off." Never "HUGE SALE" or "MASSIVE DISCOUNT."
- **Tone shift:** slightly more active — the sale is an occasion, not a clearance. Urgency comes from natural scarcity (member window, limited bottles) not manufactured countdowns.
- **Overrides:** this is the one email type where the offer can be the subject line's lead — as long as the register stays refined.

### Re-engagement / Win-Back
- **Structure:** gentle observation opening ("It's been a while…") → what's new since → member benefit teaser → invitational question → "Explore What's New" CTA.
- **Copy approach:** focus on what they're missing, not where they've been. "Your cabinet has been waiting" — playful personification, zero guilt. List 3–5 specific new arrivals using arrows.
- **Tone shift:** gentle, curious, forward-looking. Never guilt-tripping ("we miss you"), never desperate ("come back for 30% off!!").

### Cart Abandonment
- **Structure:** gentle reminder hook → product callback (with image) → natural scarcity if applicable → proactive shipping reassurance (arrows) → offer of help → CTA.
- **Copy approach:** address the likely objection before the reader names it — shipping safety is the big one for expensive whisky. Lead with care, not pressure.
- **Tone shift:** service-forward. The voice is helpful, not nagging. "We're holding it for you" beats "Your cart is about to expire."
- **Overrides:** no discount in email 1 of the sequence. Discount in a later email only if the cart is high-value or the subscriber is first-time — otherwise it trains customers to abandon for the code.

### Welcome / Onboarding (flow)
- **Structure:** email 1 — warm welcome + brand value prop + invitation to explore. Email 2 — member benefits / how to shop. Email 3 — educational content (reading labels, regions, cask types). Email 4 — first-purchase nudge with genuine value (free shipping reminder or modest member-only offer).
- **Copy approach:** make the subscriber feel they made a smart choice. Establish expertise and exclusivity early — but deliver it with warmth.
- **Tone shift:** appreciative and welcoming without being effusive. "Thank you for joining The Really Good Whisky Company" — simple, warm, done. Not "we're SO excited you're here!!"

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **"Whiskies" — plural.** Single most important rule. _Always_ singular "whisky." Catches this brand voice every time when it slips.
- **"Nerd" / "nerdy" addressing customers.** Internal language only. Externally: "connoisseurs," "whisky enthusiasts," "Really Good Club members."
- **"Elevate."** Overused marketing-speak. Also ban: "game-changer," "must-have," "essential."
- **"Grab," "snag," "tons of," "super cheap."** Register-breaking casualness.
- **Aggressive sales language.** "BUY NOW," "DON'T MISS OUT," "LIMITED TIME ONLY" — kills the premium positioning on contact.
- **Generic luxury clichés.** "Indulge yourself," "treat yourself," "pamper." The voice doesn't beg; it invites.
- **Competitor bashing.** Never "nobody else is doing this." The brand positions on what it is, not what others lack.
- **Pun piles.** Wordplay is one drop, not a flood. "Whisky Business: A Toast-ally Amazing Dram-tastic Sale" is the anti-example.
- **Corporate preamble.** "We hope this email finds you well," "Dear Valued Customer," "We are pleased to announce" — generic, distant, wastes the opening.
- **"Cordially invite you to peruse"–style formality.** Refined is the goal; starched is the failure mode.

_Universal banned moves (AI-ese, fake urgency, weasel hedging, generic e-com adjectives, etc.) apply automatically — see `references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

| Segment | Voice adjustment |

| Really Good Club members | Insider register. "Members get first dibs." Frame offers as member-first access, everyone-else-tomorrow. |

## Design References

- **Voice references:** Apple (luxury + accessibility, premium design with warmth), Mr Porter (editorial British tone, sophisticated without stuffy), John Lewis (premium-but-friendly British retail cadence).
- **Avoid sounding like:** Master of Malt (too catalog-y, too dense), Amazon (too transactional), a Scottish distillery's own site (too heritage-heavy, too formal).

## Extras

- **The 1,200-bottle number.** Use it. It's a concrete, impressive, credibility-building fact. "Over 1,200 hand-picked bottles" lands harder than "a huge selection."
- **"We treat each bottle like liquid gold."** Signature phrase for shipping/handling copy. Use in post-purchase, cart abandonment, or shipping-focused promotional emails. Never twice in the same send.
- **Emotional pillars to evoke:** discovery & delight ("uncover," "rare find"), confidence & pride ("makes guests pause mid-conversation"), exclusivity & belonging ("first access, 24 hours before anyone else"), trust & assurance ("fully insured, expertly packed").
- **Content mix (roughly):** 40% discovery/exclusivity (new arrivals, limited releases), 30% education/expertise (guides, tasting notes, regions), 20% community/membership (Really Good Club benefits), 10% promotions/value (sales, free shipping).
- **Humour boundaries.** Never self-deprecating (undermines premium positioning). Never at the customer's expense. Never about getting drunk or irresponsible consumption. British wit is a knowing wink, not a belly laugh.
