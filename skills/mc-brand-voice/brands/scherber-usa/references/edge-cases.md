# Scherber USA — Edge Cases & Voice Boundaries

Companion file to `brand.md`, `copy-samples.md`, and `lexicon.md`. Read this before handling anything off-pattern: price pushback, "I'm not trained" objections, a brief that wants to run a flash sale, a founder-story angle, a tragedy in the news, or any send where the easy move (discount, fear, hype) would be the wrong one.

Five sections:
1. **Anti-paragons** — copy that's clearly *not* us, with diagnoses. Scan before writing to pre-empt the common failure modes.
2. **Objection handling** — the three recurring customer objections and the canonical Scherber response to each.
3. **Dual-audience balancing** — how to write one email that lands with a trained EMT and a concerned parent without alienating either.
4. **Edge cases & defaults** — discount policy, fear/tragedy moments, founder-story handling, humor, topics to avoid.
5. **Don't confuse our voice with…** — comparisons to adjacent brands and categories. Useful when a draft starts drifting.

## 1. Anti-paragons — copy that's definitely NOT us

### Anti-paragon 1: Hype-y promotional welcome

> *Subject: OMG! Welcome to the Scherber family! 🎉*
>
> *Hey there, friend!*
>
> *We're SO EXCITED to have you join our AMAZING community of prepared people! You're going to LOVE our incredible products!*
>
> *Right now, we're offering an EXCLUSIVE 20% discount just for new subscribers! Don't miss out on this limited-time offer!*

**Why this is wrong for us:**
- Forbidden words stacked: *amazing, incredible, exclusive, don't miss out, limited-time.*
- Emoji in subject line — never appropriate for this brand.
- Exclamation points throughout — the voice is confident; confidence doesn't shout.
- "Friend" — too casual for a brand selling equipment that saves lives.
- Leads with a discount instead of with value or credibility.
- Vague claims ("love," "excited") instead of concrete benefits or trust markers.

### Anti-paragon 2: Fear-based educational email

> *Subject: Is your family in danger?*
>
> *Most people don't realize how unprepared they are until it's too late.*
>
> *When disaster strikes, will you be ready? Or will you watch helplessly as tragedy unfolds?*
>
> *Every year, thousands of people die from preventable injuries because they didn't have the right supplies. Don't let your family become a statistic.*

**Why this is wrong for us:**
- Fear-mongering subject line — not our posture.
- Fear language throughout: *too late, tragedy, die, statistic, watch helplessly.*
- Focuses on what the reader lacks instead of what we solve.
- Manipulative and guilt-inducing.
- Directly against our solution-focused, confidence-building approach. We build confidence, not anxiety.
- An EMT reading this tunes out; a concerned parent feels cornered. Both bad outcomes.

### Anti-paragon 3: Vague cart abandonment

> *Subject: Come back! We miss you! ☹️*
>
> *Hi there,*
>
> *We noticed you left something in your cart and wanted to reach out. We'd love to help you complete your purchase!*
>
> *Our products are really great and we think you'd be super happy with them. Plus, we're offering free shipping right now!*
>
> *Hope to see you soon!*
>
> *Warmly,*
> *The Scherber Team*

**Why this is wrong for us:**
- Emotional manipulation ("we miss you," sad emoji) — Scherber never begs.
- Vague language ("really great," "super happy") — no concrete value props.
- Doesn't address the actual objection (price, completeness, uncertainty about which kit).
- Free shipping buried at the end rather than stated clearly.
- "Warmly" sign-off is wrong register — too casual for a brand selling emergency equipment.
- "We noticed you" / "wanted to reach out" — consultant-speak we explicitly reject.
- No trust marker. No credibility. Nothing that justifies a purchase decision.

## 2. Objection handling

The three objections that recur across the funnel. Each has a canonical Scherber response — learn the move, not the exact words.

### Objection 1: "It's too expensive / I could build my own cheaper"

**Approach:** Don't apologize for price. Don't compete on cost. Reframe price as the *complete, organized, field-tested* package versus the hidden cost of DIY (vendor hunting, guesswork, missing supplies). Value justifies price; we never discount our way into a "yes."

**Canonical response:**
> *Building your own kit means sourcing from multiple vendors, researching what you need, and hoping you didn't miss anything critical. Every Scherber kit is complete, organized, and ready — saving you time and ensuring nothing is missed.*

**What not to do:** Offer a discount to close the objection. Apologize with *"we know it's a big investment, but..."* as preamble to a price justification. Compete on cost by listing what's cheaper than competitors.

### Objection 2: "Do I really need all this / Seems like overkill for me"

**Approach:** Acknowledge the dual-audience reality. Emphasize preparedness as responsibility, not paranoia. Don't minimize the seriousness of emergencies, but also don't escalate the reader into fear. *"Hope you never need it. Be ready if you do."* is the canonical line.

**Canonical response:**
> *Whether you're a trained EMT or simply want reliable emergency equipment at home — serious injuries require serious supplies. Hope you never need it. Be ready if you do.*

**What not to do:** Tell them they definitely need the professional kit. Lead with fear scenarios ("what if your child..."). Strip down the product into something lesser to match perceived need — instead, show how the complete kit serves both use cases.

### Objection 3: "I don't know how to use this equipment"

**Approach:** Validate the concern without over-selling the difficulty. Recommend training (Stop the Bleed, basic first aid) as the honest next step. Position quality equipment as the *first* step, not the only one. Mention HSA/FSA eligibility if relevant — it signals legitimacy and lowers the barrier.

**Canonical response:**
> *Quality equipment is the first step. We also recommend basic first aid or Stop the Bleed training to use these supplies confidently. But having the right equipment ready is essential.*

**What not to do:** Claim the kit is "so easy anyone can use it" — dishonest and undermines the product. Ignore the concern and push the sale. Exaggerate the difficulty to drive training referral affiliate revenue.

## 3. Dual-audience balancing

Scherber writes for a trained first responder and a concerned parent in the **same email, same line, often the same sentence.** Getting this wrong is the single most common way Scherber copy goes off.

**The rule:** The technical term earns credibility with the professional; the plain-English pairing or category teaches the civilian. Neither audience is alienated. Neither is talked down to.

**Working pattern:**
> *Bleeding control supplies for traumatic injuries — tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, pressure dressings.*

The professional recognizes "hemostatic gauze." The civilian understands "bleeding control supplies for traumatic injuries." Both reach the next line.

**Signal phrases that do both jobs at once:**
- *"Whether you're a trained professional or simply preparing your family..."* — explicit bridging.
- *"The same equipment professionals use"* — civilian gets reassurance; professional gets recognition.
- *"Departments asked for..."* — launch framing that positions the professional as the driver and gives the civilian a social-proof reason to trust it.
- *"Trusted by 1,000+ departments"* — professional trust marker that reassures the civilian without patronizing them.

**How the drift happens:**
- **Drifting civilian-only:** Softening everything, dropping brand names, explaining what a tourniquet is in-line, adding reassurance about "you can do this." Reads as consumer-casual. The EMT closes the email.
- **Drifting professional-only:** Jargon-stacking (ALS, hemostatic, exsanguination) without a plain-English anchor. The civilian feels outside the conversation.

**When to go single-audience:**
- **Pure B2B departmental send** — the whole list is first responders. Drop the civilian pairings; use trade language freely.
- **Civilian-only segment** (e.g., home preparedness, family-focused campaign) — still include specific equipment names (that's what proves it's not a generic kit), but lean on context pairings more heavily.
- **Default:** dual-audience. Assume the list is mixed.

## 4. Edge cases & defaults

### Discount policy — when offers are allowed, when they're not

Scherber doesn't compete on price. But *some* offer structures are on-brand; the rule is whether they preserve or cheapen the product.

**On-brand offers:**
- **Free shipping** — never cheapens the kit itself. See Sample 4 (Promotional: Free Shipping) for the canonical "Same… Same… Same… Just…" framing.
- **Bulk / department pricing** — positions Scherber as serious about B2B. Always present as *"departmental pricing,"* not *"save money."*
- **Seasonal** (Black Friday, year-end) — allowed, but framed as factual deadline, not manufactured urgency.
- **HSA/FSA eligibility** — not a discount but reads as one to the customer. Surface where relevant.
- **New customer offer** — if running one, keep it dignified (free shipping, small credit) and don't lead with it.

**Never-allowed offers:**
- **Flash sale / countdown urgency** — breaks the voice completely.
- **Percentage-off as the lead** — price first, value second is the wrong order.
- **"Last chance" / "Don't miss out"** — manipulation language.
- **Win-back discounts in re-engagement** — we let subscribers go with dignity. No "we'll pay you to come back."
- **Cart abandonment discounts** — no. Trust markers and completeness close the sale. (Small exception: free shipping as a subtle nudge is OK.)

### Fear, tragedy, or real-world emergencies in the news

- **Default:** don't reference directly in marketing sends. Scherber sells preparedness, not current-event anxiety.
- If a scheduled send conflicts with a major tragedy (mass-casualty event, natural disaster), **delay the send.** Do not rewrite the copy to reference it — the reference itself reads as opportunistic even when well-meant.
- If the timing of a send would look insensitive (e.g., a fire-department kit promo the week of a high-profile LODD), delay.
- Exception: if a real-world incident drives a *product* decision (departments asking for vehicle kits after a specific event), the framing in Sample 5 — *"We heard you. Departments asked for..."* — is allowed. Let the customer drive, not the incident.

### Founder / company-history content

- **Default:** keep focus on products and customer needs. Don't build the brand around the founder story.
- If a founder email is sent, it's short, product-anchored, and signed with a first name (*— Justin,* *— the Scherber team*). Not a long origin story.
- Company awards, certifications, or press mentions: only surface if they directly support trust (HSA/FSA approval, ISO certification of a supplier, professional endorsement). Skip if it's just vanity marketing.

### Humor

- **None.** Emergency equipment is serious. We don't joke about injuries, emergencies, preparedness, or equipment failure.
- Even light tone-shift moments (casual mentions, asides) stay within the professional register. No puns. No "we see you reaching for the tourniquet" winks.
- The one place warmth shows up is through customer quotes and testimonials — *their* voice can be lighter than ours.

### Topics to avoid or de-emphasize

- **Graphic injury scenarios** — never describe wounds, bleeding, or trauma in vivid detail. We speak about emergencies with gravity, not drama.
- **Discount-led campaigns** — see discount policy above.
- **Competitor naming** — don't name competitors in copy. Our differentiators (completeness, authentic equipment, organization) speak for themselves.
- **Political content** — Scherber's audience spans first responders, military, parents across the political spectrum. Don't anchor to any partisan frame.
- **Statistics-first fear framing** — *"34,000 people die every year from..."* is off-brand. We don't sell from preventable-death statistics.

### When the brief is vague

- Default to **educational / value-add tone** rather than promotional. Leading with teaching builds trust even if the campaign later pivots to product.
- If the audience isn't specified, default to **dual-audience.**
- If the offer structure isn't clear, ask one clarifying question. Don't guess percentages or deadlines.
- If the product angle isn't clear, default to the core completeness story (*"complete, organized, ready"*) over a narrower feature.

### Personal sign-off vs. brand sign-off

- **Brand sign-off (default):** *Scherber USA* or *The Scherber Team.* Use for promotional, educational, product launch, cart abandonment, re-engagement.
- **Personal sign-off (first name):** use when the email genuinely is from a person — founder welcome, customer service follow-up, a specific operational message. Signed *— [First name]* or *Thanks, [Name].*
- **Never use:** *Warmly, Cheers, Best regards, XOXO.* See lexicon §8.

## 5. Don't confuse our voice with…

- **Budget first-aid brands (generic drugstore kits, Amazon Basics first-aid):** they sell a kit cheap. We sell a kit that's *complete and authentic.* Never compete on price; compete on contents and trust.
- **Tactical / military aesthetic brands (5.11, Condor, high-speed-low-drag messaging):** they sell an identity. We sell equipment. Borrow their respect for professionalism, not their aspirational-warrior tone. Our customer includes parents and nurses, not just operators.
- **Medical supply B2B (Bound Tree, Henry Schein):** they speak to procurement officers in spec-sheet language. We speak to the person who'll use the kit — whether that's a department's medical officer or a parent in a car. Same technical accuracy, more human framing.
- **Preppers / survivalist brands:** they sell from fear of collapse. We sell from readiness for the everyday-serious. *"Hope you never need it. Be ready if you do"* is the line; *"when society collapses"* is not.
- **Direct-response emergency brands with hype-heavy marketing:** countdown timers, "doctor-endorsed" claims, fake scarcity. Our trust markers are real (1,000+ departments, 50,000+ kits, authentic CAT and HyFin). Never simulate urgency.
- **Premium-luxury aesthetic brands:** we're not selling a lifestyle. We're selling equipment for critical moments. The voice is serious and clean, but not aspirational or indulgent. No "elevated." No "curated experience."
- **"Mommy blogger" home-preparedness content:** warm and first-person, often centered on "as a mom..." framing. Our civilian audience includes parents, but we don't write *as* parents. We write *for* them — with the authority of the first responders the parent trusts.
