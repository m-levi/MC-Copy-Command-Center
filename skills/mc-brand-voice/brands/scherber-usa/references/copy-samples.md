# Copy Samples — Scherber USA

**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-20

Annotated exemplars of Scherber's strongest sends. When writing new copy for Scherber, read these first. Patterns here override abstract rules when the two conflict.

The six samples cover the full email spectrum Scherber runs — welcome, educational, cart abandonment, promotional, product launch, and re-engagement. Each shows a different tone shift while holding the same voice: authoritative, solution-focused, dual-audience-aware.

---

## Sample 1 — Welcome Sequence (flow)

**Subject line:** Welcome to Scherber
**Preview text:** Complete trauma kits trusted by first responders nationwide

**Body:**
```
Thanks for joining.

Scherber builds complete trauma kits trusted by first responders nationwide. Every kit includes authentic equipment, expert organization, and everything you need—pre-packed and ready.

Whether you're a trained professional or simply preparing your family, we focus on complete solutions. No missing supplies. No assembly. Just ready.

You'll hear from us about once a week with equipment guides, preparedness tips, and new products.
```

**CTA:** Browse Complete Kits

**Why this works:**
Opens with "Thanks for joining." — three words, direct, zero fluff. The second paragraph establishes *who we are* and *who trusts us* in one sentence, then names three value props in the next. "Whether you're a trained professional or simply preparing your family" is the dual-audience move in its simplest form — it signals that this brand speaks to EMTs and concerned parents simultaneously. The close is the signature formula: *No missing supplies. No assembly. Just ready.*

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Opened with "Hey there, friend!" or an emoji-laden exclamation ("🎉 Welcome to the Scherber family!"), led with a discount code, and used "amazing/incredible" instead of naming actual equipment.

## Sample 2 — Educational: Trauma vs. First Aid (campaign)

**Subject line:** What belongs in a trauma kit
**Preview text:** Serious emergencies need different equipment

**Body:**
```
Most first aid kits are built for minor injuries. Cuts. Scrapes. Headaches.

But serious emergencies require different equipment:

**Bleeding control** – Tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, pressure dressings
**Airway management** – Airways, masks, rescue barriers
**Wound care** – Trauma pads, chest seals, Israeli bandages

That's the difference between a first aid kit and a trauma kit. One handles minor situations. The other handles critical moments.

Every Scherber trauma kit is built for serious emergencies—with supplies professionals trust and organization that saves time.
```

**CTA:** See Trauma Kits

**Why this works:**
80% education, 20% product. The opener lands with sentence fragments — *Cuts. Scrapes. Headaches.* — which gives the paragraph its rhythm and lets the next sentence ("But serious emergencies require different equipment") hit harder. Uses technical terminology (tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, chest seals, Israeli bandages) but pairs each with a plain-English category (Bleeding control, Airway management, Wound care) — that's the "industry terms with context" rule in practice. The product plug at the end doesn't sell; it positions.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Used fear framing ("Is your family in danger?"), stacked statistics about preventable deaths, and led with the product instead of the distinction. Scherber teaches the difference — it doesn't scare the reader into buying.

## Sample 3 — Cart Abandonment (flow)

**Subject line:** Your kit is waiting
**Preview text:** Complete supplies. Nothing missing. Ready when you are.

**Body:**
```
You left a kit in your cart.

We get it—emergency equipment is an investment. But here's what you're getting:

Complete supplies. Nothing missing.
Organized compartments. Fast access.
Authentic equipment. Built to last.
Same-day shipping. Ready when you are.

First responders across the country trust Scherber. You can too.
```

**CTA:** Complete Your Order
**Follow-up line:** Questions about which kit is right for you? Reply to this email.

**Why this works:**
Direct acknowledgment in line one — no "we noticed you were shopping with us" consultant-speak. "We get it — emergency equipment is an investment" does the job of a discount without being a discount. The four value-prop lines use matched two-part rhythm (*Complete supplies. Nothing missing. / Organized compartments. Fast access.*) which becomes its own drumbeat. "You can too" is the trust bridge — it extends the first-responder credibility to the civilian reader without being patronizing.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Offered a 10% discount to close the sale ("Come back! Here's 10% off!"). Scherber holds the line on price — trust and quality do the closing.

## Sample 4 — Promotional: Free Shipping (campaign)

**Subject line:** Complete trauma kits—free shipping this week
**Preview text:** Same authentic equipment. Same expert organization. No shipping charge.

**Body:**
```
You asked for it: Free shipping on all complete kits through Sunday.

Same CAT tourniquets, HyFin chest seals, and authentic equipment.
Same research-backed organization.
Same pre-packed, ready-to-go approach.

Just no shipping charge.
```

**CTA:** Shop Complete Kits
**Deadline line:** Free shipping ends Sunday, October 26.

**Why this works:**
This is Scherber's version of a promotional send — and it shows why the brand can run promotions without cheapening. The offer leads, but it's framed as *"you asked for it"* — a response to customer need rather than a manufactured push. The "Same... Same... Same... Just..." formula is the voice doing the work: the quality hasn't changed, only the shipping cost. Every word earns its place. No "HUGE SALE!!!" no countdown. The deadline is factual, not urgent.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"🔥 LIMITED TIME: FREE SHIPPING — DON'T MISS OUT!" with a countdown graphic. Scherber never runs that email.

## Sample 5 — Product Launch: Vehicle IFAK (campaign)

**Subject line:** New: Vehicle IFAK with bleeding control
**Preview text:** Compact. Complete. Rip-away mounting.

**Body:**
```
We heard you.

Departments asked for a vehicle-mounted kit with serious trauma supplies. Something compact enough for a headrest but comprehensive enough for critical moments.

The new Vehicle IFAK includes CAT tourniquet, chest seals, and trauma supplies—plus the same organized, pre-packed approach you expect from Scherber.

Rip-away mounting. Grab and go. Ready.
```

**CTA:** See the Kit
**Specs line:** 95+ supplies • MOLLE-compatible • Ships same day

**Why this works:**
*"We heard you."* — two words, big impact. This is a launch email that positions the customer (specifically: departments) as the driver of the product decision. That's the credibility move in one line. The design-challenge setup — *"compact enough for a headrest but comprehensive enough for critical moments"* — names a real tradeoff and tells the reader how we solved it. Specific equipment names (CAT tourniquet, chest seals, MOLLE-compatible) signal legitimacy to the trained audience; the plain descriptors (Rip-away mounting. Grab and go. Ready.) make it accessible to the concerned parent. Specs use bullet separator (•), not commas — a Scherber format quirk that reads as technical.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Opened with "We're so excited to introduce our newest product!" Scherber doesn't announce excitement — it delivers solutions.

## Sample 6 — Re-engagement (flow)

**Subject line:** Still interested in emergency preparedness?
**Preview text:** We haven't heard from you in a while

**Body:**
```
We haven't heard from you in a while.

If you're still interested in complete trauma kits and emergency equipment, we're here. If not, we understand.

Trusted by 1,000+ departments. Complete kits. Ready when you need them.
```

**CTA:** Browse Kits | Update Preferences | Unsubscribe

**Why this works:**
The most restrained send in the playbook. "We haven't heard from you in a while" is the whole acknowledgment — no guilt, no "we miss you," no emoji-frown. "If not, we understand" is the voice of a brand confident enough to let the reader go. All three options are given equal weight — Browse, Update, Unsubscribe — which reads as respect rather than obligation. The trust marker ("1,000+ departments") is the close, not the hook.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"Come back! We miss you! ☹️ Here's 20% off to welcome you back!" Scherber never begs. Never discounts its way back into a customer's inbox.

## Anti-paragon 1 — Overly promotional welcome

**Subject line:** *OMG! Welcome to the Scherber family! 🎉*

**Body:**
```
Hey there, friend!

We're SO EXCITED to have you join our AMAZING community of prepared people! You're going to LOVE our incredible products!

Right now, we're offering an EXCLUSIVE 20% discount just for new subscribers! Don't miss out on this limited-time offer!
```

**Why this fails for Scherber:**
- Forbidden words stacked: *amazing, incredible, exclusive, don't miss out, limited-time.*
- Emoji in subject line — never appropriate for this brand.
- Exclamation points throughout — the voice is confident; confidence doesn't shout.
- "Friend" — too casual for a brand selling equipment that saves lives.
- Leads with the discount instead of with value or credibility.
- Vague claims ("love," "excited") instead of concrete benefits.

## Anti-paragon 2 — Fear-based educational email

**Subject line:** *Is your family in danger?*

**Body:**
```
Most people don't realize how unprepared they are until it's too late.

When disaster strikes, will you be ready? Or will you watch helplessly as tragedy unfolds?

Every year, thousands of people die from preventable injuries because they didn't have the right supplies. Don't let your family become a statistic.
```

**Why this fails for Scherber:**
- Fear-mongering subject line — not our posture.
- Fear language throughout: *too late, tragedy, die, statistic.*
- Focuses on what the reader lacks instead of what we solve.
- Manipulative and guilt-inducing.
- Directly against Scherber's solution-focused, confidence-building approach. We build confidence, not anxiety.

## Anti-paragon 3 — Vague cart abandonment

**Subject line:** *Come back! We miss you! ☹️*

**Body:**
```
Hi there,

We noticed you left something in your cart and wanted to reach out. We'd love to help you complete your purchase!

Our products are really great and we think you'd be super happy with them. Plus, we're offering free shipping right now!

Hope to see you soon!

Warmly,
The Scherber Team
```

**Why this fails for Scherber:**
- Emotional manipulation ("we miss you," sad emoji).
- Vague language ("really great," "super happy") — no concrete value props.
- Doesn't address the actual objection (price, completeness, uncertainty about which kit).
- Free shipping buried at the end rather than stated clearly.
- "Warmly" sign-off is too casual for the brand register.
- No trust marker. No credibility. Nothing that justifies a purchase decision.

## Common patterns across high-performing Scherber sends

Notice these recurring moves across Samples 1–6:

1. **Opening with problems, acknowledgments, or direct statements** — never "Hope you're well!" Always hooks with relevance in the first line.
2. **Sentence fragments for rhythm** — *Cuts. Scrapes. Headaches.* / *Rip-away mounting. Grab and go. Ready.* Creates momentum without urgency language.
3. **The "No X. No Y. Just Z." formula** — *No missing supplies. No assembly. Just ready.* Appears in welcome, product, and brand summary contexts. A signature move.
4. **Trust markers woven naturally** — *1,000+ departments, field-tested, authentic equipment, CAT tourniquet, HyFin* show up in body copy contextually, not as banner claims.
5. **Specific equipment names** — CAT, HyFin, Israeli bandage, MOLLE. Legitimacy to professionals; educational detail to civilians.
6. **Action-focused, specific CTAs** — *Shop Complete Kits, See Trauma Kits, Complete Your Order, See the Kit.* Never *Learn More* or *Click Here.*
7. **No exclamation points** — even in promotional sends. Confidence doesn't need emphasis punctuation.
8. **Dual-audience awareness** — emails work for the trained EMT (*CAT tourniquet, departments*) and the concerned parent (*family, preparedness, ready*) simultaneously. Neither is alienated.
