# Scherber USA — Lexicon

The working vocabulary for Scherber copy. Hero words do the credibility work; forbidden words break the serious-but-accessible frame this brand depends on.

When in doubt, ask: would an EMT skim this and trust us? Would a concerned parent understand it without feeling talked down to? If either answer is no, rewrite.

---

## 1. Hero words

Reach for these. They are the load-bearing vocabulary of the brand.

- **Complete / comprehensive** — the primary differentiator. Every kit comes complete. Use as often as the sentence earns it. Pair with what "complete" means in context (no assembly, no missing supplies, pre-packed).
- **Authentic / proven / trusted / field-tested** — credibility words. Applied to equipment, brands, and outcomes. "Field-tested" beats "professional-grade" because it shows proof; "authentic CAT tourniquet" beats "high-quality tourniquet" because it names the brand.
- **Professional / professionals / departments / first responders / agencies** — the credibility audience. "Trusted by first responders nationwide" is our single most-used trust marker; use it naturally, not as a banner slogan.
- **Ready / organized / pre-packed** — the readiness triad. Almost always appears together or adjacent. "Pre-packed and ready" is a reusable phrase.
- **Designed / curated / research-backed** — design-intent words. Use when naming *why* a kit contains what it does.
- **Critical moments / emergencies / serious trauma** — stakes language. Never "disaster," never "tragedy." We speak about emergencies with gravity, not drama.
- **Equipment / supplies / gear** — product-category nouns. Interchangeable. All three are professional-register.
- **Reliable / dependable** — outcome words. Used for the civilian audience more than the professional.
- **Safe / safety** — the peace-of-mind angle. Used when addressing civilians.
- **Quality** — used with restraint. *Quality equipment* works; *quality products* reads as filler. Anchor it to a noun that carries weight.
- **Built for / designed for** — framing phrase. *Built for critical moments.* *Designed for real emergencies.*

## 2. Forbidden words

Zero tolerance. Each is banned for a specific reason.

- **Cheap, affordable, budget-friendly, economical** — we do not compete on price. Using these words signals the wrong brand. Scherber is full-price, value-driven.
- **Amazing, incredible, awesome, revolutionary, game-changing, unbelievable** — hype words with no substance. An EMT reading "amazing trauma kit" tunes out instantly.
- **Limited time, don't miss, hurry, act now, last chance, flash sale** — urgency tactics. Scherber's offer structure is factual (*free shipping ends Sunday, October 26*), never manufactured.
- **Sale, discount, deal** — avoid unless genuinely relevant (bulk pricing, seasonal, HSA/FSA). Never lead with any of them.
- **Best (without context)** — we don't claim "the best." We show trust markers (1,000+ departments, 50,000+ kits sold).
- **Designed by EMTs** — use *"professionally designed"* or *"research-backed"* instead. The phrase has been flagged by the client as a specific substitution.
- **Disaster, tragedy, catastrophe, die, statistic (as in "don't become one")** — fear-based language. Scherber sells preparedness, not fear.
- **Friend, buddy, folks, pals, family (as in "Scherber family")** — over-casual register. Breaks the professional frame.
- **OMG, LOL, emoji in general** — wrong brand entirely.
- **Warmly, cheers, xoxo** — sign-off register is wrong. See §4.
- **We miss you, come back, we noticed you** — manipulation language for re-engagement and cart abandonment. Scherber is direct.
- **Touch base, reach out, circle back** — corporate/consultant-speak. "Reply if you have questions" is the Scherber version.

## 3. Industry terms — use with context

Scherber writes for two audiences in the same email: the trained first responder who recognizes technical terms, and the civilian who doesn't. The rule is: use the term, then pair it with a plain-English category.

- *"Bleeding control supplies for traumatic injuries"* — not just "bleeding control."
- *"Advanced life support equipment including airway management"* — not just "ALS equipment."
- *"Tactical gear for gunshot wounds and severe trauma"* — not just "tactical gear."
- *"Tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, pressure dressings"* grouped under *"Bleeding control"* — the category teaches the civilian; the specifics validate to the professional.

When the audience is explicitly departmental (B2B), the pairing is optional. When the audience is mixed or unknown, the pairing is required.

## 4. Signature phrases

The voice's identity lives in these recurring moves. Use them when they earn their place; don't force.

- **"No assembly. No missing supplies. Just ready."** — the completeness formula. Works in emails, product copy, and taglines.
- **"No X. No Y. Just Z."** — the underlying pattern. Swappable for any three-beat claim: *No hunting. No guessing. Just grab and go.*
- **"Complete. Organized. Ready."** — three-word rhythm. Taglines, CTAs, summary lines.
- **"Built for critical moments."** — headline-register gravitas. Works as a subject line, a tagline, a closing line.
- **"When it matters most" / "When it matters"** — implies seriousness without fear-mongering.
- **"Trusted by first responders nationwide"** — primary trust marker. Use often, but naturally — not as a header slogan on every email.
- **"Ready when you need them" / "Ready when it matters"** — the peace-of-mind line. Works for civilian audience especially.
- **"Same X. Same Y. Same Z. Just [the offer]."** — the promotional formula (from Sample 4, Free Shipping). Lets Scherber run offers without cheapening: *Same CAT tourniquets, HyFin chest seals, and authentic equipment. Same research-backed organization. Same pre-packed, ready-to-go approach. Just no shipping charge.*
- **"We heard you."** — responsiveness opener (Sample 5, Vehicle IFAK). Two words; big impact when the next line names who drove the product decision.
- **"Hope you never need it. Be ready if you do."** — civilian objection-handling line.
- **"You asked for it: [the offer]"** — promotional opener that frames the offer as a response, not a push.

## 5. Specific equipment brand names (use verbatim)

Professional audiences recognize specific brands. Using the actual brand name signals legitimacy to the EMT and teaches the civilian at the same time.

- **CAT tourniquet** — full name: Combat Application Tourniquet. First reference: *authentic CAT tourniquet* or *CAT tourniquet.* Never "CAT-style" or "tourniquet" alone when we mean this.
- **HyFin chest seals** — North American Rescue product. First reference: *HyFin chest seals.*
- **Israeli bandage** — the emergency bandage. Common usage in field. Lowercase "bandage."
- **MOLLE / MOLLE-compatible** — modular webbing system. All caps. No hyphen in the acronym.
- **IFAK** — Individual First Aid Kit. All caps. Expand on first mention if the audience is mixed: *"Vehicle IFAK (Individual First Aid Kit)."*
- **North American Rescue** — manufacturer reference. Full brand name.
- **Stop the Bleed** — training program. Title case. Used when recommending training.

## 6. Trust markers (use numerals)

All numbers as numerals. These are credibility anchors — they must land exactly, not be paraphrased.

- **Trusted by 1,000+ departments** — the primary departmental trust marker.
- **50,000+ kits sold** / **10,000+ orders** — the scale marker (check which is current before use).
- **200+ supplies** — kit-specific completeness marker.
- **95+ supplies** — Vehicle IFAK marker.
- **8 compartments** — organization marker.
- **Ships same day** — operational marker. Always three words, never "ships the same day."
- **Assembled in USA** — manufacturing marker.

## 7. Name treatment

- **Brand name:** *Scherber USA* on first mention in formal sends, standalone product pages, and site hero. *Scherber* acceptable in second reference and in short-form contexts.
- **Never:** *SUSA,* *Scherber USA Team* as a signature voice, *The Scherber Company,* *Scherber, Inc.* in customer-facing copy.
- **Product names:** sentence case with proper nouns preserved. *Basic First Responder Trauma Kit. Premium Trauma Kit with Bleeding Control. Vehicle IFAK.*

## 8. Sign-off conventions

- **Corporate emails:** *Scherber USA* or *The Scherber Team.* Plain, no comma preface.
- **Personal emails (founder/team member):** First name only (*— Justin* or *— the Scherber team*).
- **Never use:** *Warmly, Cheers, Best regards, Sincerely yours, XOXO.* Too casual or too corporate — both break the register.

## 9. Customer pronouns

- **How we address the reader:** *You.* Second person, direct. Never *friend, buddy, folks.*
- **How we refer to ourselves:** *We / our* — the brand and team. Use *I* only if a founder or named team member is signing personally.
- **Never:** *The Scherber Family, Team Scherber, our community* (reads as consumer-casual, wrong register for a brand selling emergency equipment).

## 10. Formatting micro-rules

- **Numbers:** numerals always (*200+ supplies,* not *two hundred supplies*). Exception: narrative prose ("*one package,* *first responders*") where the word reads more naturally.
- **Specs line format:** bullet separator with padding: *95+ supplies • MOLLE-compatible • Ships same day.* Not commas.
- **Oxford comma:** yes, always.
- **Em dashes:** preferred over parentheses for emphasis. One or two per email. Use the em dash character (—), not double-hyphens.
- **Exclamation points:** rarely. Never in subject lines. Never in product descriptions. One per email maximum, and only when it genuinely belongs (a customer testimonial, a singular moment).
- **Capitalization:** sentence case throughout — subject lines, headlines, body, CTAs. Proper nouns and product/brand names preserve their capitalization (CAT, HyFin, MOLLE, IFAK, Scherber).
- **All caps:** banned. Never for emphasis, never in CTAs, never in subject lines.
- **Bullets:** for specs, lists of features, kit contents. Not for emotional or narrative body copy.
- **Bold:** sparingly. For the category label in educational lists (e.g., *Bleeding control*) or for a brand name inline. Never bold full sentences.
