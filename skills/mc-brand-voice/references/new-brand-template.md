===============================================================================
AUTHORING PROCESS (do before writing)
===============================================================================

1. CLIENT INTAKE — 30-minute call minimum. Ask:
   - Who is this brand for? (audience, not demographics)
   - What's the one-line positioning?
   - Who are the voice references? (not "stylish" — actual brand names)
   - Who are the design references? (actual brand names)
   - Any words/phrases you refuse to use?
   - Any phrases that are "us"? (signatures, recurring language)
   - Who speaks for the brand? (founder voice, brand voice, mixed)

2. SAMPLE COLLECTION — gather before writing:
   - 10-20 recent emails the brand has sent (good AND bad — both teach)
   - 5-10 social captions
   - Website hero + about copy
   - Any podcast, interview, or video where the founder talks about the brand
   - Competitor emails (for the "what we are NOT" axis)

3. RESEARCH — 1-2 hours:
   - Reddit: search brand name, brand category, competitor names
   - Reviews: Trustpilot, Google, Amazon if applicable
   - YouTube: unboxings, reviews, founder interviews
   - The founder's personal social accounts (often more revealing than brand)

4. DRAFT — use this template, aim for 400-700 lines total.

5. CLIENT VALIDATION — send the draft. Ask two questions:
   - "Does this sound like us?"
   - "What's missing?"

6. STRESS-TEST — write 3 sample emails from the draft. If they come out bland
   or off-brand, the voice section is too generic. Sharpen before finalizing.

===============================================================================
QUALITY BAR (what "done" looks like)
===============================================================================

- A junior copywriter unfamiliar with the brand could read this file and
  produce on-brand copy without asking follow-up questions.
- Every rule is enforceable — a reviewer can point at copy and say
  "this violates rule 3."
- Nothing in the file overlaps with universal-banned-moves.md.
- No section says "TBD" or is conspicuously thin.
- The Voice section passes the sharpness test: swap the adjectives for a
  competitor's and the file shouldn't still make sense.

===============================================================================
LENGTH BUDGET

Total: 200-700 lines is the sweet spot.
- Under 200: probably too thin. Voice won't hold under varied email types.
- Over 900: probably bloated. Push detail into references/ files.

A minimal brand (e.g., FatSwag — image-and-headline driven, short rules,
short subject lines) lands at the low end. A verbose brand (e.g., RGW —
long-form founder POV, distinct syntactic moves, multiple flow types)
lands at the high end. Both are fine.

Brand.md is the handoff profile. Deep content (copy banks, vocab lists,
checklists) belongs in the brand's references/ folder.

## Voice

<!-- GUIDANCE — Voice
This is the most important section. If this is sharp, the rest writes itself.

LENGTH: strict format below. Do not expand into paragraphs.

REQUIREMENTS:
- Exactly 4-6 adjectives on "Is"
- Exactly 4-6 anti-adjectives on "Is not"
- Exactly 1 sentence for "Core tension"
- One short paragraph at the end capturing the voice in a single image

CORE TENSION is where voice lives. Examples:
- FatSwag: "serious but fun" (Swag's word: busy)
- Adina: "luxurious but never pretentious"
- RGW: "premium but warm, witty but not smug"

If you can't find the tension, interview the founder again.

## Form & Format

<!-- GUIDANCE — Form & Format
DEFAULT: bullet list, each item 1-2 lines. Be numerical where possible.

FILL EVERY FIELD EXPLICITLY. Silence causes interpretation drift.
"Standard capitalization" is a real answer. "Emojis: never" is a real answer.
But don't omit the field.

EXPAND when warranted. If a brand's identity lives in a particular syntactic

  RGW uses (parentheticals) as the primary delivery vehicle for wit. Short,

  device tires. Never as exposition; only for the joke.

FIRST PERSON is where many brands leak — be specific about when "I" or "we"
is allowed, and who the "I" is.

## Email Types

<!-- GUIDANCE — Email Types
LENGTH: 4-8 email types. Each entry: 3-6 lines. Tight playbooks, not essays.

ONLY include email types the brand actually runs. Don't pad with types that
are theoretically possible.

VOICE CONSTANT, TONE SHIFTS. This is the key frame. The brand voice is the
same across all emails. What varies by email type is tone — the mood the
voice takes on for the moment. A win-back can't land in the same tone as
welcome. A post-purchase can't sound like a campaign.

EACH TYPE SHOULD INCLUDE:
- Structure (sections / modules that appear)
- Copy approach (what the copy does here specifically)
- Tone shift (WHEN APPLICABLE) — how the voice adjusts for this context
- Overrides (WHEN APPLICABLE) — rules from the general voice this type breaks

Tone shifts are most relevant for flow emails — the customer is in a specific
emotional/transactional moment that the broadcast voice doesn't naturally
land in. Examples:

  - Welcome flow: warmer, slightly more first-person than campaigns
  - Abandoned cart: matter-of-fact, not desperate
  - Post-purchase: gratitude tone, no selling
  - Win-back: candid, slightly self-aware ("it's been a minute")
  - Replenishment: helpful, almost service-tone

If the email type uses default brand voice, omit the "Tone shift" line.
Don't pad.

- Weekly/seasonal campaign
- Product launch or drop
- Sale / promo
- VIP / exclusive access
- Event or holiday
- Abandoned cart (flow)
- Welcome (flow)
- Post-purchase (flow)
- Browse abandonment (flow)
- Win-back (flow)
- Replenishment (flow, if applicable)
-->
