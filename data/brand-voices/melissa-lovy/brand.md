# Melissa Lovy

**Source:** migrated from client-provided brand voice guide + Mordi's campaign context notes (LeLe collab, Miller Studs, Cameron Necklace launches, Lovy Ladies community)
**Status:** canonical — pending copy-samples validation (starter set in `references/copy-samples.md` is DRAFT until real sends are confirmed)
**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-20

## Essence

Affordable luxury jewelry with a fine-jewelry look, written in the voice of Melissa talking to her Lovy Ladies over coffee.

## Audience

**A woman, 25–45, who wants fine jewelry without the fine-jewelry price tag.** She follows Melissa on Instagram for the fashion-meets-motherhood content, watches the stories, comments on the family posts, and buys the pieces named after Melissa's kids (LeLe, Miller, Cameron). She calls herself a "Lovy Lady" without irony. She wants the piece that looks like $800 but cost $180, and she wants to feel smart about the choice — not defensive about it.

She reads emails on her phone between carpools. She responds to personal, warm, "stylish best friend" energy. She does not respond to formal, corporate, or gatekeeping tone. She is emotionally responsive — the word "obsessed" is in her vocabulary, and when Melissa uses it about a new piece, she reads it as genuine. The brand lives inside Melissa's voice; removing the influencer warmth collapses the brand.

**List size and engagement:** active Klaviyo list with high engagement on personal and collab-story sends. Community buys into family-angle narratives (pieces named after Melissa's kids, collab launches tied to other influencers' families). Flash sales and limited-drop collabs outperform evergreen campaigns. SMS list is warm — Lovy Ladies expect personal-voice SMS.

Segmentation splits (voice shifts per segment):

- **Lovy Ladies core (warm, engaged):** full first-person Melissa voice. Family stories, personal anecdotes, behind-the-scenes. "Obsessed" lands as authentic here.
- **Newer subscribers:** Melissa voice, slightly less in-group shorthand. Explain what LeLe or Miller refers to on first mention.
- **SMS list:** Melissa voice compressed. Warm, one emoji max, short. Feels like a text from Melissa, not a marketing blast.

## Voice

- **Is:** warm, conversational, indulgent, playful, confident, intimate
- **Is not:** corporate, cheesy, gatekeepy, restrained, formal, aspirational-from-a-distance
- **Core tension:** _Luxury feel with best-friend warmth — premium without pretension, enthusiastic without being a sales pitch._

**Chatting with your stylish best friend.** Melissa is an influencer, not a corporate spokesperson. The voice is her voice — the one her Instagram followers recognize from her Stories about her kids, her style tips, her "I can't stop wearing this" moments. The copy should feel like she texted you about the launch, not like a brand wrote a product announcement. Warm, a little indulgent, a little playful, always specific about how the piece will make the customer feel and look.

## Writing Principles

1. **Sound like Melissa, not like a brand.** The copy is always in her voice — even when the sign-off says "The Melissa Lovy Team." If a line reads like a corporate email, it's wrong. Read drafts as if Melissa were saying them aloud on Instagram. If she wouldn't say it, rewrite.

2. **Story always — with an angle on how the piece makes her life better.** Every send has a narrative. Not a product description — a story. Melissa wearing the Cameron Necklace to date night. The LeLe collab born from a conversation with her kid. The Miller Studs she slept in because she forgot they were there. Product benefits come through story, not bullet-point specs.

3. **Short and punchy — 10–15 words per sentence, max.** Melissa doesn't monologue. She texts. She posts. She gives you the thing and moves on. Long, descriptive sentences are fine when the moment calls for it (product reveal, story beat) but the default cadence is short and breathable.

4. **Benefit before feature — and the benefit is emotional.** Not "18K gold plating, hypoallergenic, handmade." That's a feature list. The benefit is how the piece will make her feel: confident in the school pickup line, a little more put-together at the Zoom meeting, like she got away with something at the dinner party. Specs support the story — they don't lead it.

5. **FOMO without begging.** Real scarcity is real — small batches, limited collabs, pre-order windows. State it with warmth, not pressure. "Gone in a day last time" is real. "DON'T MISS OUT FINAL HOURS" is begging. Urgency lives in Melissa's honest enthusiasm, not in manufactured countdown theater.

6. **Apple-like headlines.** Short, confident statements. "The one you've been waiting for." "Your new favorite studs." "Meet Cameron." Never questions, rarely exclamations. The headline does the work of making her stop scrolling.

7. **Treat-yourself energy, no apology.** Melissa doesn't apologize for the purchase and the customer shouldn't have to either. "Go ahead, you earned it" is on-brand. "It's a splurge, but..." is not. Affordable luxury means the price point is already the answer — the copy never has to justify it.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** short and punchy sentences, mixed with occasional longer descriptive lines for story beats. 10–15 words is the default sentence ceiling. Paragraphs 1–3 sentences. Fragments acceptable for rhythm ("Obsessed. You will be too.").
- **Capitalization:** Apple-style Title Case for headlines ("Your New Favorite Studs"). Sentence case for body copy. Product and collection names capitalized as named (LeLe, Cameron, Miller, Lovy Ladies). Brand name always *Melissa Lovy* in full.
- **Length:** promotional emails 100–200 words (shorter than most other brands — the copy density is low on purpose). Product launches 150–250. Subject lines 5–8 words. SMS under 160 characters.
- **Emojis:** allowed sparingly — 1 per email maximum, only when it genuinely adds personality. Heart emoji, sparkle, rarely a sunglasses smile. Never a fire emoji, never a money-bag, never a siren. The rule isn't "avoid emojis" — it's "use them like Melissa uses them in an Instagram caption." **(Override to universal default.)**
- **First person:** "I" voice allowed and encouraged when the email is framed as from Melissa directly (launch announcement, behind-the-scenes, personal pick). "We" for brand-voice sends (sale copy, collection announcements). Sign-off default is "Love, Melissa" or "xo, Melissa" for personal-voice sends; "The Melissa Lovy Team" for brand-voice sends.
- **Punctuation quirks:** exclamation points allowed in moderation — 1–2 per email max, and only when the excitement is real (launch, restock, personal moment). Em dashes welcome for a beat. Ellipses used sparingly for effect ("You know the one..."). Oxford comma yes.

### The Melissa-in-first-person move

The highest-performing sends are the ones that feel like they came from Melissa's phone, not the brand's Klaviyo account. "I have been wearing these studs every single day and I had to tell you." "Can we talk about the Cameron Necklace for a second?" This register breaks the formal register that most brands use. Use it on launches, personal picks, collab reveals, and behind-the-scenes sends. Not every email needs it — but every campaign calendar should have a few.

### The family-and-community thread

Pieces are named after Melissa's kids. Collabs with other influencers often feature pieces named after their kids. The LeLe collection, the Miller Studs, the Cameron Necklace — these names carry stories the community already knows. Copy should lean into these stories as a connection device, not as a selling point. "LeLe picked out the color" beats "The LeLe Necklace comes in rose gold."

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** 5–8 words. 40–55 characters.
- **Tone:** warm, personal, specific. Occasional exclamation point for genuine launch excitement.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case default; Title Case acceptable for major launches.

**Good (real client examples / closely representative):**

- *"I'm obsessed with these"*
- *"Meet Cameron ✨"*
- *"Your new favorite studs"*
- *"The one everyone's been asking for"*
- *"LeLe just dropped"*
- *"Treat yourself, you earned it"*
- *"Back in stock — and going fast"*
- *"This is not a drill, Lovy Ladies"*

**Bad (do not send):**

- *"SHOP THE SALE NOW!!! 🔥🔥🔥"* — all caps, multiple emojis, multiple exclamations, manufactured urgency. Every move wrong.
- *"Discover our new collection of fine jewelry"* — corporate brand speak. Melissa doesn't "discover"; she shares.
- *"Premium quality 18K gold-plated earrings inside"* — leads with feature instead of feeling. Not a story, not a moment.
- *"Are you ready for our biggest launch yet?"* — question in subject line, corporate-announcement cadence. Feels like a press release, not a text from Melissa.
- *"Don't miss out on this limited drop!"* — begging urgency. Scarcity is real; Melissa states it as fact, she doesn't plead.

## CTAs

Melissa Lovy's CTAs are short, warm, and specific. They imply the customer is in on the moment.

**Approved:**

- *Shop the Collection*
- *See LeLe*
- *Meet Cameron*
- *Add to Jewelry Box*
- *Make It Yours*
- *Get the Details*
- *Shop the Drop*
- *Treat Yourself*

**Banned:**

- *Buy Now* (standalone) — too generic, too transactional. Always name what she's shopping.
- *Click Here / Learn More* — marketing-speak filler.
- *Don't Miss Out / Last Chance / Hurry* — begging urgency. Universal ban stands.
- *Grab Yours / Snag One* — cheapens the register and breaks premium.

## Email Types

### Product launch (campaign)

**Structure:** personal-voice opener from Melissa ("I've been wanting to tell you about this"). Story about how the piece came to be or how she's been wearing it. Product image. 2–3 lines of product detail woven into story (not listed as specs). Single CTA. Personal sign-off (*Love, Melissa*).
**Copy approach:** story-first. The launch is a moment, not an announcement. "Meet Cameron" beats "Introducing the Cameron Necklace."
**Tone shift:** most personal of all email types. First-person Melissa throughout.

### Collaboration launch (campaign)

**Structure:** intro to the collab partner and the story (often a family angle — pieces named after the partner's kid). Partner's voice may appear in a quoted or parenthetical moment. Product reveal. Scarcity note ("small run, as always"). Single CTA.
**Copy approach:** community-forward. The collab is a friendship made visible. Frame it as two stylish women making something together, not as a brand partnership.

### Flash sale / limited-time promo (campaign)

**Structure:** warm opener naming the moment ("Weekend sale — 25% off sitewide, here's why"). Reason for the sale when possible (birthday, holiday, restock clearance). 2–3 product callouts with short benefit notes. Clear deadline. Single CTA.
**Copy approach:** warm and honest. Melissa framing a sale is different from a brand framing a sale — she says what's on and why. **Universal ban on manufactured urgency stands; genuine time-bound scarcity (the sale actually ends Sunday) is allowed and expected.**
**Overrides:** the offer can lead the opener on flash sales. Benefit reminder comes in sentence two.

### Pre-order / small-batch drop (campaign)

**Structure:** "You know the piece I've been teasing" opener, reveal of the piece, pre-order window stated plainly, scarcity ("small batch, always sells out"), single CTA.
**Copy approach:** insider-moment framing. The pre-order is for the Lovy Ladies who have been watching. Reward the intimacy.

### Welcome (flow)

**Structure:** Email 1 — Melissa introduces herself and the brand in her voice. Email 2 — behind-the-scenes: why the brand exists, how pieces are named, what "Lovy Ladies" means. Email 3 — her top three picks with personal notes.
**Copy approach:** warm and personal from first send. Orient the new subscriber into the community, not just the product line.
**Tone shift:** first-person Melissa throughout. "I'm so glad you're here" register.

### Abandoned cart (flow)

**Structure:** "Still thinking about [piece]?" opener, quick benefit reminder, quality markers (18K gold plating, hypoallergenic, handmade), single CTA back to cart, reply-for-help secondary.
**Copy approach:** warm and helpful, not pushy. "Want to see it on?" with a video link or styling photo is strong. No discount in Email 1; small free-shipping nudge in Email 2 acceptable.
**Tone shift:** service tone — Melissa-helping-her-friend register.

### Post-purchase (flow)

**Structure:** warm thank-you from Melissa, care notes ("here's how to keep your gold plating looking new"), invitation to tag @melissalovy on Instagram, community nod.
**Copy approach:** gratitude and community. No cross-sell. Make her feel welcomed into the Lovy Ladies.
**Tone shift:** warmest of all flows. Personal sign-off.

### Win-back (flow)

**Structure:** light acknowledgment of the gap, what's new since she was last here (new collections, collabs, restocks), soft CTA.
**Copy approach:** honest and personal. "Hey, it's been a minute — here's what's new" is on-brand. "We miss you" crosses into theatrical.
**Tone shift:** candid, warm. First-person Melissa preferred.

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **Corporate brand speak.** *Discover, unlock, introducing, we are pleased to announce, leading provider, premium quality.* Melissa doesn't speak this way. Every corporate-announcement phrase breaks the friend register.
- **Over-apologetic pricing.** *It's a splurge but, worth the investment, you deserve a treat just this once.* Melissa's affordable-luxury positioning means the customer doesn't need permission. "Go ahead, you earned it" lands; "even though it's a splurge" doesn't.
- **Gatekeeping language.** *For the discerning woman, exclusive for those who appreciate fine craftsmanship, for women who value elegance.* Melissa doesn't gatekeep. Everyone who wants to be a Lovy Lady is one. Aspirational-from-a-distance framing is wrong register.
- **Feature-lead product descriptions.** *18K gold plating, hypoallergenic, handmade, unique design.* These specs are true and matter — but they support the story, not lead it. Lead with the story ("I slept in them and forgot they were there"), specs come second.
- **Stale influencer tropes.** *Bestie, queen, girl boss, #blessed, slaying.* The voice is Melissa talking to her friends — not 2016 lifestyle-influencer slang. Modern warm register, not dated catchphrase stack.
- **Cold product-spec subject lines.** *18K gold-plated hoops now available.* Reads as B2B announcement. Subject lines carry feeling, not inventory notices.
- **Over-explaining obvious emotional beats.** *Jewelry makes women feel confident. Accessories complete an outfit.* Skip the setup — go straight to the specific, personal moment ("I wore these to drop-off and got three compliments before I got back to my car").

_Universal banned moves apply automatically — see `mc-brand-voice/references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

**Exceptions to universal rules — explicit overrides for this brand:**

- **Emojis allowed sparingly** (1 per email max, aesthetic ones only: ✨, 💛, heart, occasional 😎). Universal default is "emojis off by default." Melissa Lovy specifically overrides because the brand lives inside Melissa's Instagram voice, where a sparkle emoji or heart reads as authentic warmth rather than hype. Multiple emojis per line or hype emojis (🔥, 💸, 🚨) remain banned.
- ***Obsessed* is an approved word in Melissa's voice.** Universal default flags it as influencer-hype. For this brand, "obsessed" is a signature — used when Melissa genuinely can't stop wearing something. Overuse (three "obsesseds" per email) drains the word. One per email is the ceiling.
- ***Must-have* is approved.** Universal default flags it. For Melissa Lovy, "your new must-have stud" is on-brand. Use sparingly — not every piece is a must-have, which is why the word still works when used.
- ***Treat yourself* is approved and encouraged.** Universal default flags it as indulgence-as-manipulation. For Melissa Lovy, it's permission granted by a friend — authentic to the voice. "Go ahead, treat yourself" lands because Melissa means it.
- **Real, time-bound urgency is allowed** on flash sales and limited drops. The sale actually ends Sunday; the batch actually sold out in a day last time. State it plainly. Manufactured urgency ("LAST CHANCE FINAL HOURS") stays banned.

## Extras

**The Melissa-first-person voice is the brand.** Unlike most MoonCommerce brands where the founder voice is an occasional accent, Melissa Lovy leans heavily on first-person Melissa. Many campaigns are structured as Melissa-to-friend emails rather than brand-to-customer announcements. The sign-off is *Love, Melissa* more often than *The Melissa Lovy Team.* This is a brand-defining choice — don't neutralize it to fit generic best practices.

**Family-and-community storytelling as product DNA.** Pieces are named after Melissa's kids (LeLe, Miller, Cameron) and after collab partners' kids. The LeLe collab involves Melissa's daughter picking out colors. The Cameron Necklace story includes why Melissa named it that. This is not incidental — it's the narrative scaffolding of the whole brand. Never strip the family story to tighten copy; the story is what makes the purchase meaningful.

**Mordi's preferences from past sessions:**

- Mordi consistently requests revisions to make copy "more high-end," shorter, or "more on brand" — meaning less promotional, more authentic-Melissa.
- Short, scannable copy. 10–15 words per sentence max.
- Unique CTAs — never generic phrases.
- Problem-agitation-solution frameworks, customer testimonials, lifestyle positioning, and family-perspective angles are all on-brand.
- Copy should sound natural, not cheesy. If a line feels like a template, it probably is.

**Why the brand permits universal-banned words.** Melissa Lovy is the only MoonCommerce brand where "obsessed," "must-have," and "treat yourself" are on-brand. This is because the brand lives inside an influencer voice where those words are part of the honest vernacular — removing them collapses the voice. Other brands (Daniella Faye, Adina Las Vegas, Kosher Casual, Scherber USA, etc.) still have these words banned. Never blend Melissa's permitted vocabulary into copy for other brands.

**Why the brand permits real-scarcity urgency.** Flash sales, limited collabs, and small-batch drops are genuinely time-bound at Melissa Lovy — the sale actually ends, the batch actually sells out. Stating those realities is fine. Manufactured urgency (countdown timers, "LAST CHANCE," fake "only 3 left!" banners) remains off-brand. The difference is honesty.
