# Daniella Faye

## Essence

Premium boutique modest fashion that trusts the product to speak for itself. Voice ref: Apple having coffee with your stylish sister.

## Audience

**A woman, 30–60+, Modern Orthodox Jewish, who has arrived.** She's past the trend-chasing phase. She dresses for herself, her work, her community — not for Instagram. She buys fewer, better pieces and can feel the difference between $40 modest basics and $150 Turkish-fabric craftsmanship. She is pregnant, nursing, or past both; she is a mother of young kids or a rebbetzin who buys three to five of a piece she loves at a time. She shops Jerusalem storefronts, Five Towns boutiques, Teaneck trunk shows, and the Efrat flagship. She trusts the designer-customer relationship — she knows Daniella's name, she knows Dave answers customer service, she knows the pieces are small-batch and that's part of the point.

Her price tolerance is premium but never flashy. A $180 swing dress is normal. She is not looking to be told the brand is luxury — she assumes it, and any overselling pushes her away. She values quality over quantity, generous sizing (the brand runs generous — she sizes down), nursing-friendly design, and the ability to skip the shell-and-layering strategic planning.

**List size and engagement:** active Klaviyo list, 2–4 sends per week. Strong response to small-batch launches, color drops, and sub-brand releases (Color Collection, Warehouse). Customers reply to emails. Open rates hold on quiet, confident sends — they drop when the copy pushes too hard.

Segmentation splits (voice shifts per segment):

- **Israel list (daniellafaye.com):** Jerusalem / Efrat / Ramat Bet Shemesh customers. In-community shorthand available. Shabbos, Yom Tov references land naturally.
- **US list (daniellafayeusa.com):** Five Towns / Teaneck / Chicago / Miami / Lakewood. Same voice, same references, same calendar — the Jewish audience is unified across both markets.
- **New subscribers / first-time buyers:** slightly more context, less insider shorthand. Explain what "no shells required" means, why we run generous.
- **SMS list:** compressed. Same confident, unhurried tone in fewer words.

## Voice

- **Is:** confident, understated, knowing, specific, warm, unhurried
- **Is not:** hype-y, cute, precious, apologetic, aspirational, trend-chasing
- **Core tension:** _Premium without ceremony — confident enough that it never has to announce itself._

**The friend with impeccable taste who shares her secrets.** She doesn't tell you something is amazing; she says "trust us on this." She doesn't oversell the fabric; she says "butter-soft." She treats your intelligence as a given. She's past "must-have" — the piece is either the one you'll wear all the time or it isn't, and she'll tell you which. The voice is the voice of a small boutique that knows its craft, knows its customer, and doesn't need to raise its volume to be heard.

## Writing Principles

1. **We don't make basic.** This is the core worldview, not a slogan to deploy. Every piece earns its place — no filler, no padding, no "basic" product descriptions. Copy inherits that philosophy: if a sentence could describe any modest brand's swing dress, rewrite it so it couldn't.

2. **Period over exclamation point.** Confidence ends sentences with periods. Excitement that needs exclamation points is excitement the copy couldn't earn with its words. One exclamation point per email maximum, and only when it genuinely belongs to the moment. Never in subject lines.

3. **Specific over generic.** "Butter-soft fabric that moves with you" beats "premium fabric." "Runs generous — most customers size down" beats "inclusive sizing." "The combination that eliminates shells, strategic layering, and second-guessing" beats "versatile separates." Whenever the copy defaults to a category word, swap it for what the piece actually does.

4. **Confident, never pushy.** We don't beg, we don't plead, we don't manufacture urgency. Scarcity is real (small batches are real) but we state it as fact — "small batches, as always" — not as a threat. "Won't last" is fine when it's true; "DON'T MISS OUT" is never fine.

5. **The customer's intelligence is assumed.** She already knows quality matters. She already knows modest dressing has pain points. We don't explain why comfort is good or why layering is annoying — we solve the problem and name the solution. Over-explaining the obvious breaks the voice instantly.

6. **Lead with the benefit, add personality.** Product descriptions start with what the piece does for her day, then add a beat of warmth or wit. "The swing dress that actually swings. Flows like you want it to, fits like it should." Benefit-first, then a little character.

7. **Modesty is the frame, not the pitch.** Our customer needs coverage — that's assumed. Never preach it, never frame it as a problem she has, never say "finally — a dress that covers." The clothes cover because that's what the brand makes. The copy talks about fit, fabric, versatility, and craft.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** mix short and medium. Short, definitive statements ("Your new go-to. Obviously.") carry weight because the surrounding sentences earn it. Never three short fragments in a row unless it's a deliberate refrain ("Small batches. Big difference."). Sentences generally 10–18 words, with the occasional shorter definitive line.
- **Capitalization:** Apple-style Title Case for headlines ("Your New Go-To. Obviously."). Sentence case for body. Product names capitalized as named (Dahlia Set, Triangle Back Top, Basic/Loose Dress). Brand name always *Daniella Faye* — never "DF" or "Daniella Faye USA" in body copy (the .com distinction is for operations, not voice).
- **Length:** promotional emails 120–250 words. Product launches 150–300. Subject lines short and confident — 5–8 words, 40–55 characters. SMS under 160 characters.
- **Emojis:** never in subject lines, never in body. Premium positioning breaks the moment an emoji appears. Hard line.
- **First person:** "we" is the default. "We don't make basic." "We're betting you'll love it." "Trust us on this." Dave and Daniella occasionally appear by name in founder-voice or customer-service contexts, but broadcast copy stays brand-voice-plural. Signature: *The Daniella Faye Team* or no sign-off.
- **Punctuation quirks:** periods over exclamation points. Em dashes are welcome for a beat of warmth or wit — sparingly, once or twice per email. Oxford comma yes. Parentheticals allowed for the insider aside ("(most customers size down)"). Ellipses avoided — they read as uncertain.

### The definitive fragment

"Your new go-to. Obviously." "Small batches. Big difference." "Smart choice." "Your wardrobe will thank you."

Daniella Faye's most recognizable voice move is the short, definitive closer — a two-to-five-word sentence that lands like a period on the whole email. It's used sparingly, at structurally weighty moments: the end of a product description, the close of an email, the headline. It works because the brand has earned the confidence to do it — overuse drains the device. One per email, maybe two if the email is long.

### The insider aside

Parentheticals and light jokes work when they feel like a friend leaning in. "(We're betting you'll love it.)" "(Most customers size down.)" "(Trust us on this one.)" They assume the reader is in on the brand. Never defensive, never apologetic — the aside adds warmth without breaking premium.

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** 5–8 words. 40–55 characters.
- **Tone:** confident, insider, specific. Never questions. Never exclamation points. Never urgent.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case for most sends; Title Case acceptable for major launches ("The Dahlia Set Is Here").

**Good (real client examples / closely representative):**

- *"Small batch alert: The set everyone's asking about"*
- *"Just dropped. Won't last."*
- *"The Dahlia Set is here"*
- *"We don't make basic"*
- *"Your new go-to. Obviously."*
- *"Four colors. Small batch. Back in stock."*
- *"Quality you can feel"*
- *"The difference is in the details"*

**Bad (do not send):**

- *"OMG you HAVE to see this!! 🔥"* — emoji, all caps, exclamation points, and manufactured hype. Every move is wrong.
- *"Ready to feel amazing?!"* — questions and exclamation points break the confident register. "Amazing" is banned.
- *"Last chance! Don't miss out on the Dahlia Set!"* — manufactured urgency and begging tone. Scarcity is real; we don't have to beg.
- *"The perfect dress exists!"* — "perfect" is banned; exclamation point is banned; the claim reads as influencer-pitch.
- *"Must-have modest essentials inside"* — "must-have" and "essentials" both break premium; "modest" in subject lines narrows the pitch.

## CTAs

CTAs are where voice dies fastest. Daniella Faye's CTAs imply ownership and confidence — never urgency, never generic.

**Approved:**

- *Make It Yours*
- *Get the Details*
- *See What's New*
- *See the Collection*
- *Shop the Drop*
- *Find Your Fit*
- *See All Colors*
- *Explore the Color Collection*

**Banned:**

- *Buy Now / Shop Now* — generic, transactional, no brand presence.
- *Don't Miss Out / Last Chance / Hurry* — manufactured urgency, universally banned.
- *Grab Yours / Snag One / Score One* — cheapens the brand register.
- *Click Here / Learn More* — marketing-speak filler.
- *Add to Cart!* — exclamation point breaks voice; "Add to Cart" itself is fine on button copy.

## Email Types

### Product launch (campaign)

**Structure:** short benefit-first opener naming the piece and what it solves. Product image. 2–3 lines of detail (fabric, fit, versatility). "The details:" line with 3–4 bullet notes (runs generous, nursing-friendly, colors, small batch). Single CTA. Short definitive closer.
**Copy approach:** confident, specific, understated. The launch is the news — the email doesn't have to sell it hard. "Just dropped. Won't last." does more work than a paragraph of hype.

### Small-batch drop / restock (campaign)

**Structure:** "Small batch alert" or "Back in stock" opener naming the piece and colors. Product images. Scarcity stated plainly ("Small batches, as always"). Single CTA. Optional aside noting sizing or care.
**Copy approach:** the customer was waiting — state the news, close quickly. Never oversell. "The set everyone's asking about" beats "You won't believe what's back."

### Sale / promotional (campaign, infrequent)

**Structure:** clear offer in the opener, stated without urgency theater. 2–3 product examples with short benefit reminders. Single CTA. Deadline stated factually if applicable.
**Copy approach:** calm, specific, not apologetic about running a sale. Daniella Faye discounts conservatively (generally 20–30% off seasonal items or Warehouse section). Never frames sale as "don't miss out." Frames as "here's what's on right now."
**Overrides:** leading with the offer is OK on a sale send — but the benefit still lives in sentence two.

### Welcome (flow)

**Structure:** Email 1 — warm welcome + what the brand does + why it's reliable. Email 2 — product education (what makes the fabric different, what "runs generous" means, what "no shells required" means). Email 3 — customer favorites.
**Copy approach:** orient the new subscriber. She signed up for a reason. Say what we make in the first 30 words. Confidence, not sales.
**Tone shift:** slightly warmer than campaigns. More "we're glad you're here," less "here's what's new."

### Abandoned cart (flow)

**Structure:** "Still thinking?" opener, benefit reminder (2–3 sentences), sizing-help offer ("We run generous — most customers size down"), single CTA back to cart, secondary reply-for-help CTA.
**Copy approach:** matter-of-fact, not desperate. Offers help rather than pressure. No discount in email 1.
**Tone shift:** more service-y than campaigns. "We're here if you have questions" register.

### Post-purchase (flow)

**Structure:** brief warm thank-you, care note if applicable, invitation to tag @daniella.faye or reply with photos. No cross-sell.
**Copy approach:** gratitude only. Make her glad she bought. "Smart choice. Your wardrobe will thank you." is the archetype.

### Win-back (flow)

**Structure:** light acknowledgment of the gap, what's new since she was last here (new collection, Color Collection drops, Warehouse additions), soft CTA.
**Copy approach:** candid and slightly self-aware. Never "we miss you." "Here's what you've missed" is honest; it's also the answer to the question she's likely asking herself.
**Tone shift:** warmer, candid.

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **Hype superlatives.** *Amazing, incredible, stunning, fabulous, unbelievable, perfect, flawless.* These words all fail the confidence test — a premium brand doesn't need them. If the fabric is good, say what it does ("butter-soft, moves with you") instead of calling it amazing.
- **Influencer-speak.** *Obsessed, iconic, must-have, game-changer, treat yourself.* Wrong audience, wrong register. The Daniella Faye customer doesn't think of her purchases as "treats" — she thinks of them as smart choices. (Note: this is a deliberate divergence from Melissa Lovy, which is allowed to use this register. Don't blend.)
- **Affordability framing.** *Cheap, budget, affordable, accessible price.* Apologizes for premium pricing and positions the brand wrong. We don't mention price as a selling point. The price is what it is; the product justifies it.
- **Questions in headlines or subject lines.** *Ready to feel amazing? Need a wardrobe update? Looking for something new?* Breaks the confident, definitive register. Statements only.
- **Lifestyle marketing speak.** *Live your best life, elevate your everyday, essential wardrobe solutions, transcendent experience.* Corporate lifestyle language that says nothing. Daniella Faye describes the piece, not the lifestyle the piece implies.
- **Over-explaining the obvious.** "Because comfort matters." "Because you deserve quality." Don't explain why quality matters — the customer knows. Don't explain why comfort is good — she has a body. Treat her intelligence as a given.
- **Modesty as the lead pitch.** "Modest fashion that's finally stylish." "Look modest without looking frumpy." Modesty is the frame, not the headline. Frame the piece by what it does; modesty is a constraint the brand already solves.
- **Apologetic premium.** "Worth the investment." "It costs more, but..." Never. The price stands. If we have to explain why a dress is $180, the copy already failed.

_Universal banned moves apply automatically — see `mc-brand-voice/references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

## Extras

**The founder story — how it appears in copy.** Daniella Faye Farin started making jewelry at age 11 in Chicago, moved to Israel in high school and never came back, met Dave at a museum, and hand-sewed her first headbands while listening to Torah classes. She sold the Ein Sof flagship dream on Hillel Street in Jerusalem in 2015. This story is powerful but used selectively — founder-origin notes in welcome flows, founder-voice emails during holidays, storytelling sends about craft and Israeli manufacturing. It does not appear in every product email. The brand voice is confident enough to let the product lead; the founder story reinforces the craft claim when surfaced.

**"Made in Israel" as an active value.** After a production crisis where the brand worked with cheaper factories and got broken zippers, non-stretch fabric, and withheld goods, Daniella returned to the original Israeli factories — more expensive, but worth it. This shows up in copy as quiet pride, not marketing claim. "Made in Israel" can be stated once per email max, and usually as part of the craft story rather than a patriotic claim.

**Generous sizing is a known feature.** Long-time customers know the brand runs generous. New subscribers don't. Always note "most customers size down" in product descriptions, fit guides, and abandoned cart flows — it's the single biggest returns-reducer and a quiet brand differentiator.

**Dave's customer-service philosophy.** "I don't care what your price range is." The brand's customer service is famously generous — credits offered past policy, free replacements on faded pieces, flexibility beyond stated terms. This shows up in copy as confidence ("Love it or return it") rather than explicit policy theater. The generosity is the brand; the policy is paperwork.

**Color Collection and Warehouse.** Two sub-lines with their own positioning:
- **Color Collection** — mid-range ($75–80), broader color range, slightly more casual register. Voice stays Daniella Faye; content is more color-led and less craft-heavy.
- **Warehouse** — clearance section for older inventory. Voice stays calm and specific. Frame as "older seasons still available," never as a distress sale.

**Community and charity moments.** The brand has supported soldiers' families (gifted rings to soldiers' wives), run dignity-preserving refugee clothing sales, and funded community activities. When these come up in copy, they're stated plainly without grandstanding. "We gifted rings to the wives of soldiers in our community" — full stop, no congratulatory framing.
