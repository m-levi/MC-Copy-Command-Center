## Sample 1 — Dahlia Set product page (website, not email)

**Headline:** Your New Go-To. Obviously.

**Body:**
```
The top and skirt combination that actually works together. Designed to eliminate the shell, the layering, the strategic planning. Just put it on and go.

Cut from our signature butter-soft fabric that moves with you, not against you. The kind of quality that makes other clothes feel wrong afterwards.

Details:
- No shells required
- Nursing-friendly design
- Available in XS-3XL (most customers size down)
- Limited quantities per color

Size Guide Note: Pro tip: We run generous. When in doubt, size down. Trust us on this one.

Shipping Info: Get it fast. Love it longer.
Returns: Love it or return it. (We're betting you'll love it.)
```

**CTA:** Make It Yours

**Why this works:**
- The headline "Your New Go-To. Obviously." is the platonic Daniella Faye move — definitive statement, period, two-word follow-up that adds knowing confidence without adding words.
- "Designed to eliminate the shell, the layering, the strategic planning" names the actual problem in concrete terms (the strategic planning that modest women do every morning) instead of abstracting it into "versatile" or "effortless."
- "The kind of quality that makes other clothes feel wrong afterwards" sells premium without the word *premium*.
- The parenthetical insider aside — "(most customers size down)" — assumes intelligence and trades a selling move for a service move.
- CTA "Make It Yours" implies ownership rather than transaction ("Buy Now").
- Return copy uses warmth ("We're betting you'll love it") to close out the anxiety without discounting.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Generic swing-set descriptions lean on "perfect for every occasion" or "effortless elegance" — lifestyle marketing-speak that doesn't tell the customer anything specific. The Daniella Faye version names the concrete thing (no shells, no layering, no strategic planning) instead.

## Sample 2 — The Dahlia Set product launch (email, from client Copywriting Style Guide)

**Subject line:** Small batch alert: The set everyone's asking about
**Preview text:** Just dropped. Won't last.

**Body:**
```
The Dahlia Set is here. You know the one. The top and skirt that work together without you having to think about it. The combination that eliminates shells, strategic layering, and second-guessing.

We made it in four colors. Small batches, as always. Because when the fabric is this good, everyone having the same thing isn't the point.

[Product Image]

The details:
Butter-soft fabric that moves with you.
Nursing-friendly design.
Generous sizing (most customers size down).
The kind of quality you feel the moment you put it on.

[Make It Yours - CTA Button]

Smart choice. Your wardrobe will thank you.

Get it fast. Love it longer.

The Daniella Faye Team
```

**CTA:** Make It Yours

**Why this works:**
- "You know the one" is the insider move — assumes the customer has been watching the brand's content, treats her as a community member rather than a stranger.
- "When the fabric is this good, everyone having the same thing isn't the point" does three things at once: confirms the fabric quality (confident claim, no hedging), reframes scarcity as a brand value rather than an urgency tactic, and signals premium without saying *premium*.
- The four-line detail list is punchy but each line is a full sentence (except "Nursing-friendly design" — acceptable shorthand). Not catalog fragments.
- Closer "Smart choice. Your wardrobe will thank you." stacks two brand-signature short sentences. Lands like a period on the whole email.
- "Get it fast. Love it longer." — signature brand tagline working as shipping/returns shorthand in sign-off position.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
A typical boutique swing-set launch would say "Obsessed with the Dahlia Set!? 😍 Limited quantities — grab yours before they're gone!" — emoji, exclamation, manufactured urgency, begging tone. The Daniella Faye version says "Just dropped. Won't last." and lets the product carry the rest.
