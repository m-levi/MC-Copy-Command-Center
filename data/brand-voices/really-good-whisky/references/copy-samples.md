## Sample 1 — Limited Release Announcement

**Subject line:** Your next extraordinary dram has arrived
**Preview text:** A limited release from one of Scotland's most celebrated distilleries

**Body:**
```
We've just secured something rather special.

A limited release from one of Scotland's most celebrated distilleries — only 200 bottles made it to our shores, and we've managed to get our hands on 30 of them.

This isn't your everyday dram. Aged 18 years in sherry casks, with notes of dark chocolate, dried fig, and a whisper of smoke that lingers beautifully. The kind of whisky that makes you pause mid-conversation.

Really Good Club members get first dibs with 15% off. Everyone else? Act fast — these won't last the week.

Questions? Drop us a line. We're passionate about this stuff.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Explore the Collection

**Why this works:**
"Something rather special" is the defining British understatement — it reads as restraint, not hype. Specific numbers ("200 bottles made worldwide, we've secured 30") carry more credibility than any "exclusive!" could. The em dash in "these won't last the week" is the single drop of wit allowed per email. Every tasting-note detail serves a purpose — "a whisper of smoke that lingers beautifully" is sensory selling at its cleanest. Member access comes _after_ the product story, so it feels like a benefit, not a gate.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
Opened with "We're so excited to announce…" Listed "premium quality" instead of specific tasting notes. Used "whiskies" somewhere. Closed with "Don't miss out!"

## Sample 2 — Educational / Value-Add

**Subject line:** Behind the barrel: How we choose exceptional whisky
**Preview text:** Ever wondered what makes a bottle truly special?

**Body:**
```
Ever stood in front of a bottle wondering what "cask strength" actually means? Or whether "single malt" is better than "blended"?

Here's your quick guide to decoding the details:

Single Malt — Whisky from one distillery, made entirely from malted barley. Think precision and character.

Cask Strength — Bottled straight from the barrel without dilution. Bold, intense, and not for the faint of heart.

Age Statement — The youngest whisky in the bottle. A 12-year-old might contain some 15-year-old magic, but it's honest about what you're getting.

Want to dive deeper? Our team is always here to guide you to your perfect pour.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Explore the Collection

**Why this works:**
Opens with the reader's actual question, not the brand's announcement. Em dashes replace colons in the definitions — a small typographic move that reads cleaner and fits the voice. Each definition is short, confident, and carries one line of personality ("not for the faint of heart," "honest about what you're getting"). The CTA comes after the value has been delivered, positioned as "want more?" rather than "buy now." The closing line reinforces expertise-as-service, not expertise-as-gatekeeping.

## Sample 3 — Really Good Club Recruitment

**Subject line:** The insider access your collection deserves
**Preview text:** First dibs on rare releases, exclusive discounts, expert guidance

**Body:**
```
Building an exceptional whisky collection takes more than just browsing — it takes insider knowledge, early access, and trusted guidance.

That's exactly what the Really Good Club delivers:

→ First access to limited releases (24 hours before anyone else)

→ Exclusive discounts up to 20% on every order

→ Personalized recommendations from our team of passionate connoisseurs

→ Free shipping on all UK orders over £150

→ Members-only special releases and distillery collaborations

The membership pays for itself in just two purchases. And you'll join a community of whisky enthusiasts who appreciate craftsmanship, rarity, and exceptional quality.

Questions about membership? We're here to help.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Join the Really Good Club

**Why this works:**
Arrows (→) are the distinctive formatting move — no traditional bullets, no numbered list. Each benefit is specific and quantified (24 hours, 20%, £150). "Pays for itself in just two purchases" is the ROI framing a connoisseur audience responds to — concrete, unsentimental, respectful of their intelligence. The community framing ("join a community of whisky enthusiasts") lands _after_ the benefits, so belonging feels earned rather than pitched. CTA says "Join," not "Sign Up" — the register difference is deliberate.

## Sample 4 — Seasonal Promotion

**Subject line:** Spring's finest single malts are here
**Preview text:** 20% off hand-picked selections, free shipping over £150

**Body:**
```
Spring has arrived, and so has something rather lovely: 20% off a hand-picked selection of exceptional whisky.

From rare single malts to exclusive releases, now's the time to add something brilliant to your collection.

Really Good Club members can shop the sale now. Everyone else gets access tomorrow.

Free shipping on UK orders over £150.

Need a recommendation? Our team is here to guide you to your perfect pour.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Shop the Collection

**Why this works:**
This is the textbook RGW sale email — premium positioning fully intact despite the discount. "Spring has arrived, and so has something rather lovely" is elegant parallel structure, with "rather lovely" doing the British-understatement work. The offer is stated directly but never shouted. Member-first access gates the urgency naturally ("shop now" vs. "tomorrow") without any manufactured countdown. Free-shipping and the recommendation offer close the email by reinforcing service, not sale.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"HUGE SPRING SALE! 20% OFF EVERYTHING!!! LIMITED TIME ONLY!!" — everything this voice exists to reject.

## Sample 5 — Re-engagement / Win-Back

**Subject line:** Your cabinet has been waiting
**Preview text:** Here's what's arrived since your last visit

**Body:**
```
It's been a while since you've explored our collection.

Here's what's arrived since your last visit:

→ Limited Japanese releases from award-winning distilleries

→ Rare Islay single malts with beautifully complex peat

→ Exclusive independent bottlings from Scottish Highlands

We've also expanded our Really Good Club benefits — members now save up to 20% on every order.

Ready to discover your next extraordinary dram?

Our team is here to help you find your perfect pour.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Explore What's New

**Why this works:**
"Your cabinet has been waiting" is the kind of personification the brand allows — one charming line, no pun, no cleverness piled on. The opening is a gentle observation, never a guilt trip ("we miss you," "where have you been"). The "here's what's arrived" pivot is forward-looking — it tells the reader what they've been missing without lecturing them for being away. Each new-arrival bullet is specific (Japanese / Islay / Highlands) rather than generic ("loads of new whisky"). The closing question ("Ready to discover your next extraordinary dram?") invites, never pressures.

## Sample 6 — Cart Abandonment

**Subject line:** Your bottle is waiting
**Preview text:** Complete your order — fully insured shipping included

**Body:**
```
You left something rather special in your basket.

[Product image + product name]

We're holding it for you, but we wanted to mention — this is one of our limited releases, and we're down to the last few bottles.

Questions about shipping? We've got you covered:

→ Fully insured delivery worldwide

→ Premium packaging for complete protection

→ Free shipping on UK orders over £150

Need help completing your order? Drop us a line.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Complete Your Purchase

**Why this works:**
"You left something rather special in your basket" keeps the premium register even in the service context — the product is still "rather special," not "the item in your cart." Scarcity is framed gently ("down to the last few") rather than manufactured ("only 3 left!!"). The shipping reassurance block is the key move — for a £200+ whisky bottle, _the_ unspoken objection is shipping safety. The email addresses it before the reader has to bring it up. No discount in this first-touch email. The warm support offer ("Need help completing your order?") reinforces the service register.

## Sample 7 — Welcome Flow, Email 1

**Subject line:** Welcome to exceptional whisky
**Preview text:** You've just unlocked access to over 1,200 hand-picked bottles

**Body:**
```
Thank you for joining The Really Good Whisky Company.

You've just unlocked access to over 1,200 hand-picked bottles from around the world — rare single malts, exclusive releases, and exceptional drams you won't find anywhere else.

Our London-based team sources the finest whisky from British distilleries to Japanese masters, each bottle chosen for exceptional quality and character.

Whether you're building a collection, searching for that perfect bottle, or exploring the world of whisky, we're here to guide you to your perfect pour.

Need a recommendation? Just ask. We're passionate about this stuff.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** Explore the Collection

**Why this works:**
"Thank you for joining" opens simply — no effusive overreach, no "we're SO excited." The 1,200-bottle number lands immediately, doing the credibility work before any marketing language has to. "British distilleries to Japanese masters" signals global reach without a paragraph of explanation. The third paragraph holds space for all three reader postures (collector / searcher / curious newcomer) in a single sentence. Closing line "We're passionate about this stuff" lets the voice drop one register for warmth without breaking the premium frame.

## Sample 8 — Urgency / Last Call

**Subject line:** Last call: This Highland gem sells out tomorrow
**Preview text:** Fewer than 10 bottles remain

**Body:**
```
One last heads-up on this one.

The 22-year-old Highland single malt we released last week — down to single digits, and it's unlikely to return.

For those who were waiting to decide, now's the moment.

Free shipping on UK orders over £150. Fully insured delivery worldwide.

Cheers,
The Really Good Whisky Company Team
```

**CTA:** View the Bottle

**Why this works:**
The shortest send in the set — and a good demonstration that brevity is on-brand when the content earns it. "One last heads-up" is conversational without being casual. The scarcity is real and specific ("down to single digits, unlikely to return") rather than manufactured. "For those who were waiting to decide, now's the moment" respects the reader — it assumes they've been considering, rather than assuming they need to be convinced. No pun, no "last chance!!", no urgency theatre. The shipping mention is a utility line — reassurance for high-ticket buyers.

**What a less-on-brand version would have done:**
"LAST CHANCE!!! Don't miss out on this once-in-a-lifetime Highland single malt! Only a few bottles left! Buy now before they're gone forever!" — fake urgency, hype, and three punctuation sins in one breath.
