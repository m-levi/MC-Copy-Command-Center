## Essence

The Old Navy of modest — comfortable, reliable basics that make modest dressing easier, sold without hype.

## Audience

**A woman, 35+, who stopped looking for fashion a long time ago and just wants clothes that work.** Modern Orthodox Jewish, Apostolic Pentecostal, conservative Christian, Haredi entering the workforce, or a woman in the middle of becoming more observant — different communities, same problem. She needs a shell top that stays put under her dress, a skirt that won't lose its shape after ten washes, and a tee with enough length to stay tucked in when she reaches for the coffee pot. She's been burned before — bought something from a modest brand that looked great on the website and came out of the dryer a size smaller, or ordered a re-up of last year's favorite and got a subtly different cut. She wants a brand that doesn't play that game.

She's not trend-curious. She doesn't want a "modest fashion moment." She wants basics she can buy again next year and trust that they'll be the same basics. Her price tolerance is real but not aspirational — she'll pay for Turkish fabric that lasts, but she walks away fast from anything that feels overpriced or positioned as luxury.

**List size and engagement:** active email + SMS list. Strong response to restock announcements and practical educational content (fit solutions, care tips, styling tips for existing wardrobe). Sale emails work, but soft-framed stock-up promos outperform urgency-driven ones.

Segmentation splits (voice shifts per segment):

- **Jewish customers (default email list):** category terms like "modest" and "Shabbos" land naturally. Shell tops as a layering staple is understood without explanation.
- **Christian / multi-faith subscribers:** same voice, less cultural shorthand. "Modest" stays; specific holiday or community references get translated or omitted.
- **New subscribers / TikTok Shop acquisition:** warmer welcome, more explanation of what the brand makes and why the consistency matters. Assume less context.
- **SMS list:** even plainer than email. Same voice, compressed. No fluff, no emojis, no subject line.

## Voice

- **Is:** practical, direct, warm, honest, matter-of-fact, conversational
- **Is not:** luxurious, trendy, preachy, corporate, hype-y, choppy
- **Core tension:** _Quietly confident without any premium positioning._ The brand knows the product works and refuses to dress the language up to sell it — but the sentences still flow warmly, not clipped.

**A friend who solves problems.** The kind of woman who answers "where did you get that?" with "Kosher Casual, I wear it under everything, it actually stays put" — full sentence, no pitch, no hype. The brand writes the way she talks: complete thoughts, short paragraphs, one clear point per email. It never oversells and never postures.

## Writing Principles

1. **Lead with the benefit, full sentence.** Never open with the product name or a sale banner — open with what the piece does for her day. "Our shell tops stay in place from morning carpool to evening errands" beats "The shell top is back" and beats "20% off shell tops." The benefit comes first, stated in a complete sentence.

2. **Complete sentences, not fragments.** This is the biggest voice tell. "New shell tops. Soft fabric. Stays put." reads as cheap catalog copy — that's not us. "Our shell tops stay in place all day, made with soft stretch fabric that won't ride up or lose its shape" is us. Short paragraphs are fine. Choppy sentence fragments are not.

3. **Old Navy, not Nordstrom.** If a line could appear in a department-store email from Nordstrom, Neiman Marcus, or a boutique brand's "curated capsule" drop — rewrite it. Our register is accessible, grounded, everyday. The moment the copy reaches for elegance or fashion-literacy, we've drifted.

4. **Modesty is assumed, not preached.** Our customer didn't sign up to be reminded she needs to cover up. She signed up because we solve coverage problems better than anyone. Don't frame modesty as a rule she's subject to, a problem she has, or a virtue she's signaling. Frame it as a wardrobe need we meet, same as "needs pockets" or "needs to fit in a carry-on."

5. **Solve a specific problem, every send.** Every email should answer "what does this do for me?" in the first two sentences. Does it stay put? Does it stop riding up? Does it layer under something she already owns? Does it survive the dryer? Name the problem the piece solves, don't describe the piece abstractly.

6. **Honest about the brand, always.** Kosher Casual has been through operational shifts — warehouse moves, stockouts, product returns after years. When any of that is relevant in customer communication, say it plainly. "We moved warehousing to the US so orders ship faster" outperforms silence. Never hide behind vague positivity when specifics exist.

7. **Faith-agnostic voice.** The product serves multiple communities. Write copy that reads as naturally to a Christian Apostolic subscriber in Tennessee as to a Modern Orthodox shopper in Teaneck. Use "modest," skip "tznius" by default, and keep Jewish-calendar references to segmented sends.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** sentences of 10–18 words, mixed. Paragraphs of 1–3 sentences. Natural conversational flow — the copy should read as if spoken by one woman helping another woman figure out her wardrobe. Never three short clipped sentences in a row; never a single 40-word sentence alone.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case for everything except the brand name (*Kosher Casual*). Subject lines, headlines, body, CTAs — all sentence case. No Title Case headlines. No all-caps anywhere except in rare, intentional emphasis (and almost never in marketing copy).
- **Length:** subject lines 40–60 characters (6–10 words). Promotional emails 150–300 words. Educational emails up to 400. SMS under 160 characters.
- **Emojis:** never. Not in subject lines, not in body copy, not in CTAs, not on SMS. This is a hard line. Emojis break the straightforward, no-hype register.
- **First person:** "we" and "our" are the defaults. "I" is allowed only in personal-feeling founder notes or customer-service replies — never in broadcast product emails. Sign-off is "The Kosher Casual Team" or no signature at all. No single-name first-person founder voice by default.
- **Punctuation quirks:** em dashes are welcome for natural emphasis — one or two per email. Exclamation points rare, maximum one per email, never in subject lines. Oxford comma yes. Ellipses avoided (read as teasing). Numbers as numerals (20+ years, 3 colors, 15% off). Contractions used naturally (we've, they're, won't, that's).

### No-fragments discipline

The voice lives or dies on this. Every competitor in modest basics (and half of non-modest basics) writes in Instagram-caption fragments: "New shell tops. Soft fabric. Stays put." That's not us. Our sentences are complete because we're speaking, not posting. A draft that reads choppy is off-brand even if every word in it is approved. When in doubt, read the paragraph out loud — if it sounds like a list, rewrite it as speech.

### Solution-first openers

The first two sentences of every email should pass this test: if a subscriber only read the subject line and the first two lines, would she know what this email is about and what's in it for her? If she'd only know that a product name exists, the opener failed. "Our shell tops stay in place from morning carpool to evening errands" passes. "Introducing the shell top" fails.

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** 40–60 characters (6–10 words). Mobile-first.
- **Tone:** benefit-focused, conversational, practical. Never urgent, never shouty, never cute.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case. Proper nouns and brand names capitalized as always.

**Good (real sends / closely representative):**

- *"Your new favorite layering top just arrived"*
- *"Finally — basics that stay comfortable all day"*
- *"Three simple ways to make any dress modest"*
- *"Stock up on the basics you wear every week"*
- *"No more tugging: shell tops that actually stay put"*
- *"Back in stock: your go-to everyday skirts"*
- *"What makes our shell tops different"*
- *"Denim is back — and it still fits the way you remember"*

**Bad (do not send):**

- *"Elevate your modest wardrobe with luxury basics"* — uses two universally banned words (*elevate, luxury*) and positions us as premium, which we aren't.
- *"🔥 FINAL HOURS: 50% OFF EVERYTHING"* — every move wrong: emoji, all caps, manufactured urgency, discount-led.
- *"Introducing our revolutionary new fabric technology"* — *introducing* is corporate-formal, *revolutionary* is hype, *fabric technology* is too technical for our register.
- *"Must-have modest fashion essentials inside"* — *must-have*, *essentials*, and *fashion* all do damage. We don't do fashion and we don't do must-haves.
- *"Babe, you have to see this"* — influencer-speak. Wrong customer, wrong voice.

## CTAs

CTAs are where the voice usually leaks. Ours are plain, benefit-tilted, and action-oriented.

**Approved:**

- *Shop shell tops*
- *Shop the basics*
- *Find your fit*
- *See all colors*
- *Stock up*
- *Browse everyday basics*
- *Shop the sale*
- *Shop new arrivals*

**Banned:**

- *Buy now / shop now* (without specifics) — too generic, feels transactional without context. Always name *what* is being shopped ("Shop shell tops," not "Shop Now").
- *Elevate your style* — universally banned word.
- *Discover / unlock* — marketing-speak we don't use.
- *Don't miss out / last chance / hurry* — manufactured urgency, universally banned.
- *Grab yours / snag one* — cheapens the brand register.

## Email Types

### Product restock / back-in-stock (campaign)

**Structure:** opener names the product, what's back, in which colors/sizes. One or two product images. Benefit reminder (stays put, works with wardrobe, quality Turkish fabric). Single CTA. Closing with customer-service nudge if fit questions are common for this piece.
**Copy approach:** matter-of-fact. The customer was waiting for this — don't over-sell it. One email, one product, one CTA.

### Product education (campaign)

**Structure:** opener frames a specific problem (coverage, fit, layering). Two or three practical tips or use cases. Natural product mention as the solution — never as the lead. Single CTA to the relevant product. Optional P.S. with a sizing or care note.
**Copy approach:** teach first, pitch second. The email should be worth opening even if she doesn't click through. "Three ways to make any dress modest" is the archetype.
**Tone shift:** slightly warmer and more conversational. She's in a learning moment, not a buying moment.

### Sale / promo (campaign)

**Structure:** offer stated plainly in the opener. Discount terms clear and specific ("take 15% off shell tops," "use code SHELL20," "sale ends Thursday at midnight"). Two or three product examples with benefit reminders. Single CTA. No countdown, no urgency pile-up.
**Copy approach:** frame as "stock up," not "don't miss out." Sale exists because of a real reason (new colors arriving, clearing the warehouse, seasonal turnover) — state it when possible. Calm, honest, specific.
**Overrides:** the sale promo is one of the few contexts where leading with the offer is appropriate. Benefit still lives in sentence two.

### Welcome (flow)

**Structure:** 3-email sequence. Email 1: warm welcome + what we do + why we're reliable. Email 2: product education (what shell tops are, what they solve). Email 3: customer favorites.
**Copy approach:** orient the subscriber fast. She signed up for a reason. Say what we do in the first 30 words. Honest, grounded, practical.
**Tone shift:** slightly warmer than campaigns. Use "we're glad you're here" — but don't linger in pleasantries.

### Cart abandonment (flow)

**Structure:** "still thinking about your [piece]?" opener, benefit reminder (2–3 sentences), sizing-help offer, single CTA to complete the order, secondary reply-for-help CTA.
**Copy approach:** assumes good faith. Never desperate, never pushy. Acknowledges the customer may be uncertain (usually about fit) and offers real help. No discount in email 1; consider a small one by email 3 only if policy allows.
**Tone shift:** more empathetic than broadcast. Closer to customer service than marketing.

### Post-purchase (flow)

**Structure:** thank-you in a warm but brief tone, care/wash note if applicable for the piece, invitation to share feedback or tag the brand. No cross-sell.
**Copy approach:** gratitude tone. The job here is to make her glad she bought — not to sell her the next thing.

### Win-back (flow)

**Structure:** light acknowledgment of the gap ("it's been a minute"), what's been happening (new arrivals, restocks, operational improvements like faster US shipping), soft CTA.
**Copy approach:** honest and slightly self-aware. Never "we miss you" — that's theatrical. "Here's what's back" is real.
**Tone shift:** warmer, candid. If the warehouse/shipping story is relevant, this is a good place to say so plainly.

### Transparency / operational update (campaign, occasional)

**Structure:** state the change or issue in the opener (warehouse move, shipping delays, product return after years out of stock, stockout resolution). Explain what it means for the customer. Thank them for patience if relevant. Single CTA if an action is available.
**Copy approach:** this is where honest-about-the-brand shows up. When an operational rumor circulates, address it directly rather than hoping it goes away. Candid beats corporate every single time for our customer.
**Overrides:** suspends the benefit-first opener rule — the news itself is the benefit of opening.

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **Luxury or premium positioning.** *Luxury basics, premium modest, boutique essentials, curated capsule* — any phrase that places us in the high-end register. We are the Old Navy of modest and that's the whole positioning. Upward drift here dissolves the brand.
- **Fashion-trend language.** *On-trend, fashion-forward, this season's must-have, the it-girl, trendsetter.* Our customer is here precisely because she doesn't want trends. Same products, year after year, is a promise — don't contradict it.
- **Choppy fragment catalog voice.** *New arrivals. Soft fabric. Stays put. Multiple colors. Shop now.* This is the most common off-brand draft we see. Fragments read as cheap and posting-flavored. Rewrite as full sentences even if the paragraph gets marginally longer.
- **Religious or community-specific framing in broadcast copy.** *Tznius, Shabbos, Yom Tov, modesty as mitzvah* — all fine in segmented Jewish-audience sends, wrong in broadcast. Our Christian subscriber shouldn't feel adjacent. Default to *modest*, *layering*, *coverage*.
- **Modesty-as-relief framing.** *Finally — something that covers, finally a dress that actually covers, no more awkward outfit choices.* Universal ban on this framing exists — noting here because it's the single most common trap in modest-category copy. Our customer doesn't think of modesty as a problem. Don't write as if she does.
- **Body-image or appearance framing.** *Flattering, slimming, hides, smooths, tummy control.* We don't sell clothes against a woman's body. We sell clothes that fit well and work. Fit language only.
- **Over-technical fabric copy.** *Shape retention technology, moisture-wicking performance knit, four-way mechanical stretch.* If a sentence would fit in a Dick's Sporting Goods product page, rewrite it in plain language. *Soft stretch Turkish fabric that holds its shape* does the same job without the jargon.
- **Discount-led subject lines.** The discount supports the email, it doesn't carry it. *"Stock up on shell tops — 20% off"* works. *"20% OFF EVERYTHING"* does not.
- **Emojis, anywhere.** Not in subject lines, not in body, not in CTAs, not in SMS. Universal default, reiterated here because every draft wants to reach for a fire emoji on the sale send. Don't.

_Universal banned moves apply automatically — see `mc-brand-voice/references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

## Extras

**Manufacturing and warehousing story.** Kosher Casual manufactures in their own facility in Turkey — this is the source of the quality and consistency claims. Warehousing transitioned from Israel to the United States in the recent past, which is worth naming in customer communications when shipping speed or order timing comes up. Israel store remains open and serves local customers. Some older inventory still ships from Israel while newer inventory ships from the US — transparency about this beats silence.

**TikTok Shop and denim return.** Two recent wins worth knowing when planning campaigns: TikTok Shop approval opens a new channel (voice should stay consistent — the platform changes, the brand doesn't). Denim has returned after being out of stock for years — this is a customer-request fulfilled, and the copy for its relaunch should lean into the "finally, it's back, and it's the same" story, not "new and improved."

**Mordi's client-side notes.** Mordi has flagged the following preferences in past sessions:

- Simplify language over fashion terminology — avoid "wardrobe solutions," "elevated basics," "shape retention." Plain talk.
- Woman-to-woman conversational tone, not woman-to-aspirational-customer.
- Educational over promotional — fabric care, styling versatility, fit solutions, seasonal recommendations all outperform pure sales pushes.
- Honest communication — when operational rumors circulate, address them directly. This is a brand-identity move, not just good PR.

**Why the voice rules this hard on fragments.** Most modest brands write in Instagram-fragment style because their aesthetic copy budget is short and their designers want punchy text. Kosher Casual specifically is not that brand. Full sentences are a differentiator — they signal seriousness about the category, not Instagram-post-flavored catalog copy.
