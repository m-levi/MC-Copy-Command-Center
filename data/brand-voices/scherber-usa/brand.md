# Scherber USA

**Source:** migrated from `scherber-usa-brand-voice/SKILL.md` (v1) + client-approved style guide
**Status:** canonical
**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-20

## Essence

Authoritative emergency-equipment voice softened by approachability — the experienced first responder who trains new recruits.

## Audience

Two audiences, one email. Scherber speaks to professional first responders (EMTs, paramedics, fire, police, search-and-rescue, military) *and* prepared civilians (parents, business owners, outdoor enthusiasts, facility managers) in the same send. The professional needs technical accuracy and brand-name credibility (CAT tourniquet, HyFin chest seals, MOLLE-compatible). The civilian needs accessibility and reassurance without condescension. The line that holds them both: *"Whether you're a trained professional or simply preparing your family."* Done well, neither audience feels written-around. Done badly, one or both close the email.

**List size and engagement:** mid-sized DTC list with strong response on educational content (trauma-vs-first-aid, kit selection guides) and on offers framed as factual (free shipping, bulk pricing, HSA/FSA-eligible) rather than urgent. Departmental B2B inquiries arrive through cart and contact forms after credibility-led campaigns; promotional sends underperform when they lead with discount and overperform when they lead with completeness.

Segmentation splits (voice shifts per segment):

- **Default mixed list:** dual-audience voice. Industry terms paired with plain-English categories. Trust markers (1,000+ departments) carried throughout.
- **Departmental / B2B segment:** professional-only register. Trade language (ALS, IFAK, MOLLE) without civilian pairings. Pricing language is *departmental,* not *discount.*
- **Civilian-only / family preparedness segment:** still uses specific equipment names (proof we're not a generic kit), but leans more heavily on context pairings and on the "ready when you need it" peace-of-mind frame.
- **Re-engagement / lapsed:** most restrained voice in the brand. Acknowledgment without guilt. *"If not, we understand."*

## Voice

- **Is:** authoritative, approachable, solution-focused, direct, credible
- **Is not:** intimidating, hype-y, fear-based, promotional, casual, condescending
- **Core tension:** _Serious without fear, expert without intimidating._ The voice carries the gravity of emergency response without the manipulation of fear-based marketing, and the expertise of a trained professional without the gatekeeping that excludes the civilian.

**The first responder training new recruits.** Knowledgeable and direct, but never cold. Confidence comes from facts (1,000+ departments, field-tested, authentic CAT tourniquet) — not from adjectives. The voice respects the reader's intelligence: it teaches without lecturing, names equipment by brand without showing off, and trusts the product to close the sale rather than ratcheting volume to push it. When this voice runs a promotion, it sounds like the same person who taught the trauma-vs-first-aid distinction last week — not a different brand wearing a sale jacket.

## Writing Principles

1. **Lead with completeness, never with discount.** The differentiator is *complete, organized, ready* — not price. Even on a free-shipping send, the kit (CAT tourniquet, HyFin chest seals, pre-packed) leads; the offer follows. A reviewer can ask of any opener: did we see the kit, the problem, or the percent? It should be the kit or the problem.
2. **Specific over claim, always.** *"Authentic CAT tourniquet"* beats *"high-quality tourniquet."* *"Trusted by 1,000+ departments"* beats *"professional-grade."* If a sentence claims something abstract (*quality, premium, reliable*) without a concrete anchor (a brand, a number, a specific feature), revise it until it earns the claim.
3. **Problem-solution structure, not fear-solution.** Open with the problem the reader already recognizes (*incomplete kits, hunting for supplies, missing critical items*) and pivot to how Scherber solves it. Never escalate the reader into fear. *"When emergencies happen, you don't have time to hunt for supplies"* is on-brand. *"Don't let your family become a statistic"* is not.
4. **Dual-audience awareness in every line.** Industry terms (hemostatic gauze, tourniquets, ALS) pair with plain-English categories (bleeding control, airway management). The professional gets credibility; the civilian gets context. Read the email twice — once as an EMT, once as a parent with no training. If either audience falls out, revise.
5. **"No X. No Y. Just Z." is a signature move — use it when it earns its place.** *No assembly. No missing supplies. Just ready.* Three-beat fragments build the brand's rhythm. Don't force them into every email; do reach for them when the message is about completeness or readiness.
6. **Trust markers are facts, not claims.** *1,000+ departments, 50,000+ kits sold, field-tested, authentic CAT tourniquet, HyFin chest seals, ships same day, assembled in USA.* These are real and they land exactly. Don't paraphrase them. Don't pad them. Don't replace them with vague trust adjectives.
7. **Confidence does not need exclamation points.** No exclamation marks in subject lines. None in product descriptions. One per email maximum, and only when something genuine earns it (a customer testimonial, a singular moment). The voice is confident; confidence doesn't shout.
8. **When in doubt, the EMT-and-parent test.** Would a working EMT skim this and trust us? Would a concerned parent understand it without feeling talked down to? If either answer is no, rewrite.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** short to medium sentences (5–15 words). Very short sentences (1–4 words) for emphasis. Sentence fragments for rhythm — *Cuts. Scrapes. Headaches.* / *Rip-away mounting. Grab and go. Ready.* Three-part lists for closing rhythm (*Complete, organized, ready*). Active voice always. No hedging (*might, could, possibly*) — direct declarative statements.
- **Capitalization:** sentence case throughout — subject lines, headlines, body, CTAs. Proper nouns and product/brand names preserve their capitalization (*CAT, HyFin, MOLLE, IFAK, Scherber*). All-caps banned outright (reads as shouting; instantly breaks the professional register).
- **Length:** subject lines 30–50 characters ideal, 60 maximum. Promotional body 100–250 words. Educational body 200–400 words. Cart abandonment and re-engagement under 150 words. SMS under 160 characters.
- **Emojis:** never. Scherber's brand is professional and serious. No emoji in subject lines, body, CTAs, or anywhere else — including face emojis in re-engagement, fire emojis in promo, or check marks in lists.
- **First person:** *We / our* is the brand-voice default. *I* is reserved for personal sign-offs from a named team member (founder welcome, customer service follow-up). Never *I* in a brand-signed send.
- **Punctuation quirks:** em dashes preferred over parentheses for emphasis (one or two per email). Use the em dash character (—), not double-hyphens. Oxford comma yes, always. Exclamation points rare (see Rhythm above). Specs use bullet separator with padding: *95+ supplies • MOLLE-compatible • Ships same day.* Not commas.
- **Numbers:** numerals always (*200+ supplies,* not *two hundred supplies*). Exception: narrative prose ("*one package,* *first responders*") where the word reads more naturally. Trust-marker numbers must land exactly — don't paraphrase *1,000+ departments* or *50,000+ kits sold.*

### "No X. No Y. Just Z." pattern

The brand's signature syntactic move. Three-beat structure with the first two beats naming what we *don't* do (problems we solve) and the third beat naming what we *do.* Examples in canonical use:

- *No assembly. No missing supplies. Just ready.*
- *No hunting. No guessing. Just grab and go.*
- *Complete supplies. Nothing missing. Organized compartments. Fast access.* (matched two-part rhythm — variant)
- *Same CAT tourniquets, HyFin chest seals, and authentic equipment. Same research-backed organization. Same pre-packed, ready-to-go approach. Just no shipping charge.* (the promotional variant — *Same... Same... Same... Just...*)

Reach for this when the message is about completeness, readiness, or an offer that doesn't change the product. Do not force into every email — overuse cheapens the move.

### No-fear discipline

Scherber sells preparedness. Not fear. The voice does not escalate the reader into anxiety to drive a purchase. Banned framings include *don't be caught unprepared, what if someone dies, will you watch helplessly, every year thousands of people die.* The replacement framings are *complete kits, ready when it matters, hope you never need it — be ready if you do.* This is the single most important brand boundary; violating it puts the brand in the wrong category entirely.

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** 30–50 characters ideal, 60 maximum.
- **Tone:** direct and benefit-focused. Lead with the value or the outcome. Sentence case. No clickbait, no curiosity gaps.
- **Punctuation:** no exclamation points. No emoji. No all-caps. No "FW:" or "Re:" tricks.

**Good (real or representative sends):**

- *Your emergency kit is incomplete*
- *Ready when it matters most*
- *The kit first responders trust*
- *Everything you need. One package.*
- *Built for critical moments*
- *No assembly. No missing supplies.*
- *Trusted by 1,000+ departments*
- *Same-day shipping on complete kits*
- *Stop improvising in emergencies*
- *Complete trauma kits — free shipping this week*
- *New: Vehicle IFAK with bleeding control*
- *Welcome to Scherber*

**Bad (do not send):**

- *OMG! You NEED to see this! 😱* — emoji, all-caps, exclamation overload, hype register. Wrong brand entirely.
- *Flash sale ends tonight!!!* — manufactured urgency, discount-led, exclamation stacking. Scherber doesn't run that send.
- *The most amazing first aid kits ever* — vague superlative ("amazing," "ever"), no substance, no trust marker.
- *You won't believe what's inside…* — clickbait curiosity gap. Voice is direct, not coy.
- *Limited time: Save big on trauma kits* — sales-language lead, manufactured urgency. Wrong order — value should lead.
- *Is your family in danger?* — fear framing. Off-brand at the level of brand identity.

## CTAs

CTAs are where brand voice leaks fastest. Scherber's CTAs are action-focused, specific, and short — never generic, never urgent.

**Approved:**

- *Shop Complete Kits*
- *Shop Trauma Kits*
- *See Trauma Kits*
- *See the Kit*
- *Find Your Kit*
- *Build Your Kit*
- *View Equipment*
- *Complete Your Order*
- *Browse Complete Kits*
- *Browse Kits*

**Banned:**

- *Shop Now!!!* / *Buy Now!* — generic plus exclamation. Voice is calm.
- *Don't Miss Out* — manufactured urgency.
- *Grab Yours* / *Snag One* — cheapening verbs. Wrong register.
- *Click Here* — generic, gives no value preview.
- *Learn More* — vague, low-conversion. Be specific (*See the Kit, View Equipment*).
- *Get Started Today* — corporate-speak, vague.
- *Shop the Sale* — discount-led; should be product-led.

## Email Types

### Welcome (flow)

**Structure:** three-word opener (*"Thanks for joining."*), brief brand frame (who we are + who trusts us + value props), dual-audience bridging line, expectation-setting for future sends, single CTA.
**Copy approach:** trust-first. Establish credibility (1,000+ departments) and core value props (complete, authentic, organized) before any CTA. Set the cadence ("about once a week") so the subscriber knows what to expect.
**Tone shift:** slightly warmer than campaign default. Still no hype.
**Overrides:** no discount lead. The first email teaches and trusts; offers come later in the sequence.

### Educational / value-add (campaign)

**Structure:** problem statement or relatable scenario, problem amplification (1–3 lines), solution categories (often bulleted: bleeding control, airway management, wound care), product-as-solution close, single CTA.
**Copy approach:** 80% education, 20% product. Industry terms paired with plain-English categories. Sentence fragments for rhythm in the opener (*Cuts. Scrapes. Headaches.*). The product mention at the close is a natural pivot, never bait-and-switch.
**Tone shift:** teacher-mode — explanatory but never condescending.

### Promotional / sale (campaign)

**Structure:** offer framed as response (*"You asked for it: free shipping…"*), value reaffirmation (*"Same... Same... Same..."*), what's changing (*"Just no shipping charge"*), single CTA, factual deadline line.
**Copy approach:** lead with value, not discount. The kit hasn't changed; the offer is a small adjustment around the kit. Use *"Same X. Same Y. Same Z. Just [the offer]"* formula. Deadlines are factual (date certain), never countdown.
**Overrides:** none. The standard rules hold even on promotional sends — that's the point.

### Product launch (campaign)

**Structure:** customer-driven opener (*"We heard you."*), design challenge naming (*"compact enough for a headrest but comprehensive enough for critical moments"*), specific equipment names, descriptive feature line (*"Rip-away mounting. Grab and go. Ready."*), single CTA, specs line at footer (*95+ supplies • MOLLE-compatible • Ships same day*).
**Copy approach:** position the customer (often: departments) as the driver of the product decision. Name the design tradeoff and how we solved it. Specifics signal legitimacy to the professional; plain descriptors signal accessibility to the civilian.
**Tone shift:** mid-energy confident — "it's here, here's what it is, here's who asked for it." Not manic. Not announcing excitement.

### Cart abandonment (flow)

**Structure:** direct acknowledgment (*"You left a kit in your cart"*), empathetic-but-not-sappy reframe (*"emergency equipment is an investment"*), four-line value prop block (matched two-part rhythm), trust bridge (*"First responders trust Scherber. You can too."*), single CTA, optional reply-to-help line.
**Copy approach:** trust markers and completeness close the sale. No discount in email 1. Reply-to invitation handles the genuine objection (which kit is right) without dropping price.
**Tone shift:** matter-of-fact, not desperate. *"We get it"* does empathy without manipulation.

### Re-engagement / win-back (flow)

**Structure:** acknowledgment of inactivity (*"We haven't heard from you in a while"*), permission to leave (*"If not, we understand"*), trust marker close (*"Trusted by 1,000+ departments"*), three-option footer (Browse Kits | Update Preferences | Unsubscribe).
**Copy approach:** the most restrained send in the brand. Equal weight to all three exit options reads as respect, not obligation. No guilt, no "we miss you," no win-back discount.
**Tone shift:** quiet, dignified, slightly more personal than promotional but still brand-signed.
**Overrides:** trust marker can be the close rather than the hook. No CTA hierarchy — Unsubscribe shares the line.

### Departmental / B2B inquiry (campaign)

**Structure:** trade-language opener, departmental-pricing positioning, kit options that meet department-scale needs, contact path for bulk-pricing conversation, single CTA (*Request Department Pricing* or *See Department Kits*).
**Copy approach:** professional-only register — civilian pairings dropped. *ALS, IFAK, MOLLE* used freely. *Departmental pricing,* not *discount.* The frame is partnership, not consumer transaction.
**Overrides:** dual-audience rule suspended (this segment is single-audience by definition).

### Post-purchase (flow)

**Structure:** brief thank-you, ship/track info, what to do with the kit (storage placement, training recommendation, accessory suggestions), invitation to reach out with questions.
**Copy approach:** gratitude tone, no selling. The customer just bought — the brand's job now is to help them use the kit confidently. Mention Stop the Bleed or basic first-aid training where it earns its place; don't oversell training.
**Tone shift:** service-mode. Slightly warmer than promotional; still professional.

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **"Designed by EMTs."** Flagged by the client. Use *"professionally designed"* or *"research-backed"* instead. The phrasing is too narrow and has a defensive edge.
- **Discount-led campaigns.** Even when an offer is running, the kit (complete, authentic, organized) leads. Discount supports. Reverse the order and the brand reads as commodity. Free shipping framed as *"You asked for it"* is on-brand; *"BIG SALE!"* is not.
- **"Best" without context or proof.** *"Best value per supply"* with the proof beside it works. *"The best trauma kit"* is empty. We don't claim "the best" — we show trust markers.
- **Fear-based framing.** No *don't be caught unprepared, will you watch helplessly, what if someone dies, become a statistic.* Scherber sells preparedness, not fear. This is the brand's most important boundary.
- **Over-casual register.** No *friend, buddy, folks, hey there, Scherber family.* Wrong register for a brand selling equipment that saves lives.
- **Manipulation language in flows.** No *we miss you, come back, we noticed you, we wanted to reach out.* The on-brand pattern in cart abandonment and re-engagement is direct acknowledgment.
- **Corporate / consultant-speak.** No *touch base, reach out, circle back, leverage, synergy.* "Reply if you have questions" is the Scherber version.
- **Generic feature listing without trust anchors.** Listing *"high-quality tourniquet, premium bandages, professional-grade gauze"* is empty. The on-brand pattern names the brand (*authentic CAT tourniquet, HyFin chest seals, Israeli bandages*) — that's what proves we're not a generic kit.
- **Win-back discounts.** Re-engagement sends do not bribe with percentage-off. Trust marker closes the send; the subscriber chooses.
- **Cart abandonment discounts.** Trust markers and completeness close the sale. Small free-shipping nudge is OK; *"Here's 10% off to come back"* is not.
- **Graphic injury detail.** We speak about emergencies with gravity, not drama. No vivid wound descriptions, no statistics-of-the-dead framings, no graphic photography in copy.

_Universal banned moves apply automatically — see `mc-brand-voice/references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

## Design References

- **Voice references:**
  - *5.11 Tactical (technical product copy mode)* — borrow the *technical accuracy and respect for the professional reader.* Don't borrow the warrior-aspirational tone — Scherber's audience includes parents, not just operators.
  - *North American Rescue (the brand whose equipment we carry)* — borrow the *no-frills professional gravity.* Their customer is the EMT placing a department PO; ours overlaps with theirs but extends to the civilian.
  - *REI (educational content mode)* — borrow the *teach-with-respect tone* and the *"here's what you actually need"* practicality. REI doesn't fear-monger about getting lost; they teach navigation. Scherber teaches preparedness with the same posture.

- **Visual references:**
  - *Clean, technical, equipment-forward layouts.* Real product photography, not lifestyle shots. White or neutral backgrounds. Bullet-separator specs lines. Bold use of brand-name equipment imagery (CAT, HyFin) where it earns the visual trust marker.
  - *Avoid visually:* fear-imagery (graphic wounds, dramatic emergency scenes), tactical-aspirational (operator silhouettes, military aesthetics), discount banners that overwhelm product, anything that reads as bargain or panic.

## Extras

**Why this voice exists.** Emergency equipment buyers, professional and civilian alike, are pattern-trained to spot two failure modes: the cheap kit dressed up as professional, and the professional kit oversold with hype. Scherber's voice is the alternative to both — it shows the actual brand-name equipment (CAT, HyFin), names the trust markers (1,000+ departments), and trusts the buyer to recognize quality without being shouted at. The "first responder training new recruits" frame is doing two jobs: it gives the professional reader someone they recognize, and it gives the civilian reader someone trustworthy who doesn't make them feel small.

**Permissive exception — exclamation points.** One per email maximum, only when something genuinely earns it (a customer testimonial in their own voice, a singular operational moment). The default is zero. Subject lines and product descriptions never carry one.

**Permissive exception — first person.** *I* is allowed when an email is signed by a named team member (founder welcome, customer service follow-up). Never *I* in a brand-signed send (creates a "which I?" ambiguity).

**HSA/FSA as soft trust marker.** HSA/FSA eligibility is operationally a tax thing, but reads to the customer as institutional legitimacy. Surface it where it lowers the barrier — especially in civilian-audience and educational sends — without making it the lead.

**The single most common drift.** A draft that reads "fine" but generic — feature-list copy without specific brand names, trust adjectives without trust markers, "complete kits" as a phrase without the *what* behind it. The fix is always *more specific.* Name the CAT. Name the HyFin. Name the 1,000+ departments. The voice lives in those specifics.
