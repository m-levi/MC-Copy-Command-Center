# Scherber USA — Pre-Send Checklist

Run this before any Scherber send is handed off for approval. The single most common failure mode for this brand is copy drifting toward promotional hype or fear framing — catch those if nothing else.

For the deeper voice rationale, see `brand.md`, `lexicon.md`, `copy-samples.md`, and `edge-cases.md`. This file is the fast, pre-handoff scan.

---

## Vocabulary (the non-negotiables)

1. **No hype words.** Search the draft for: *amazing, incredible, awesome, revolutionary, game-changing, unbelievable.* Zero tolerance. An EMT reading "amazing trauma kit" tunes out.
2. **No urgency manipulation.** Search for: *limited time, don't miss, hurry, act now, last chance, flash sale, final hours.* Deadlines are stated factually — *"Free shipping ends Sunday, October 26"* — never manufactured.
3. **No price-competitive language.** Search for: *cheap, affordable, budget-friendly, economical.* Scherber doesn't compete on price; using these words signals the wrong brand.
4. **No "designed by EMTs."** Use *"professionally designed"* or *"research-backed"* instead. Flagged specifically by the client.
5. **No fear language.** Search for: *disaster, tragedy, catastrophe, die, statistic (as in "don't become one"), watch helplessly, too late.* Scherber sells preparedness, not fear.
6. **No over-casual register.** Search for: *friend, buddy, folks, pals, "Scherber family."* Breaks the professional frame.
7. **No manipulation lines in flows.** *We miss you, come back, we noticed you, we wanted to reach out* — none survive review. See Sample 6 and cart abandonment (Sample 3) for the on-brand patterns.
8. **No corporate/consultant-speak.** Search for: *touch base, reach out, circle back, leverage, synergy.* "Reply if you have questions" is the Scherber version.
9. **"Best" — only with context.** *"Best value per supply"* with proof is OK. *"The best trauma kit"* is not.

## Name & category terms

10. **"Scherber USA" on first mention in formal sends.** *Scherber* acceptable in second reference and in short-form contexts. *SUSA* never appears in customer-facing copy.
11. **Product names: sentence case with proper nouns preserved.** *Basic First Responder Trauma Kit. Vehicle IFAK. Premium Trauma Kit with Bleeding Control.*
12. **Equipment brand names — verbatim, lowercase accessories.** *CAT tourniquet.* *HyFin chest seals.* *Israeli bandage.* *MOLLE-compatible.* Never *CAT-style,* never *tourniquet* alone where we mean a CAT.
13. **IFAK on first mention — expand if audience is mixed.** *Vehicle IFAK (Individual First Aid Kit).*

## Voice & Tone

14. **"Experienced first responder training new recruits" — not "expert lecturing."** Reads authoritative but approachable, never intimidating?
15. **Confidence without hype.** The copy makes definitive statements backed by facts (1,000+ departments, CAT tourniquet, field-tested) rather than adjectives.
16. **Solution-focused, not fear-based.** The email frames what you *get* (complete, organized, ready), not what you lack.
17. **Dual-audience check.** Would an EMT skim this and trust us? Would a concerned parent understand it without feeling talked down to? If either answer is no, rewrite.
18. **No humor.** Scherber does not use humor in copy. Customer quotes and testimonials can be lighter; Scherber's own voice stays serious.

## Structure & Format

19. **Subject line 30–50 characters ideal, 60 maximum.** Sentence case. Never emoji. Never exclamation points. Never all-caps.
20. **Preview text extends the subject — doesn't repeat it.** 40–100 characters.
21. **Opens with a problem, acknowledgment, or direct statement — not "Hope you're well!"** First line hooks with relevance.
22. **Short paragraphs — 1–3 sentences.** Fragments for rhythm (*Cuts. Scrapes. Headaches.*) are on-brand.
23. **One CTA per email.** Clear, action-focused, 2–4 words (*Shop Complete Kits, See the Kit, Complete Your Order*). Never *Learn More* or *Click Here.*
24. **Specs line format: bullet separator with padding.** *95+ supplies • MOLLE-compatible • Ships same day.* Not commas.
25. **Oxford comma — yes, always.**
26. **Em dashes preferred over parentheses for emphasis.** Use the em dash character (—), not double-hyphens. One or two per email.
27. **Exclamation points — none in subject lines, none in product descriptions.** One per email maximum, and only when it genuinely belongs.
28. **No all-caps for emphasis.** Ever.
29. **Numbers as numerals.** *200+ supplies,* not *two hundred supplies.*

## Trust markers & specifics

30. **Trust marker present somewhere.** *1,000+ departments,* *50,000+ kits sold,* or equivalent. Woven in naturally, not pasted as a banner claim.
31. **At least one specific product detail.** CAT tourniquet, HyFin chest seals, 200+ supplies, 8 compartments — something concrete the reader can't get from a generic kit.
32. **Industry terms paired with context where the audience is mixed.** *"Bleeding control supplies (tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, pressure dressings)"* — not just "bleeding control" or just "hemostatic gauze."
33. **"Ships same day" — three words, never "ships the same day."**
34. **"Assembled in USA" — if claimed, must still be true.** Verify with Scherber operations before using.

## Offer framing

35. **Lead with value, not discount.** Even when an offer is running, the kit (complete, authentic, organized) leads.
36. **Offer stated factually, not with urgency tactics.** *"Free shipping ends Sunday, October 26"* — date certain, no countdown, no "final hours."
37. **"Same… Same… Same… Just…" formula for promo sends** preserves the value while naming the offer. See Sample 4 (Free Shipping).
38. **No discounts in cart abandonment or re-engagement.** Trust markers and completeness close the sale. Small free-shipping nudge in cart abandonment is OK.

## Sign-off

39. **Brand sign-off default:** *Scherber USA* or *The Scherber Team.* Plain, no comma preface.
40. **Personal sign-off:** first name only — *— Justin,* *Thanks, Justin.* Used only when the email genuinely is from a named person.
41. **Never:** *Warmly, Cheers, Best regards, Sincerely yours, XOXO.*

## Quick Kill List

If you find any of these in a draft, the email is not ready:

- Any emoji anywhere (subject, body, CTA, sign-off)
- Any exclamation point in a subject line or CTA
- Any *amazing, incredible, awesome, revolutionary*
- Any *limited time, don't miss, hurry, act now, last chance*
- Any *cheap, affordable, budget-friendly*
- Any *disaster, tragedy, die, statistic* (as fear framing)
- Any *friend, buddy, folks, Scherber family*
- Any *we miss you, come back, we noticed you*
- Any *touch base, reach out, circle back*
- Any "designed by EMTs" (use *professionally designed* / *research-backed*)
- Any all-caps word for emphasis
- Any "Scherber USA Team" or corporate-team-signoff
- Any *Warmly, Cheers* sign-off
- Any CTA that reads *Learn More, Click Here, Shop Now!!!*
- Any P.S. used as artificial urgency

## Tone-Shift Safety Checks

Voice stays constant. Tone shifts per email type. Confirm the send matches its assigned tone:

- **Welcome / onboarding:** direct, trust-first, dual-audience bridging. Opens with three words ("Thanks for joining"). No discount lead.
- **Educational / value-add:** 80% teaching, 20% product. Industry terms with plain-English pairing. Product mention is a natural pivot, not a bait-and-switch.
- **Promotional:** value leads, offer follows. "Same… Same… Same… Just…" formula is the canonical move. No countdown timers, no urgency manufactured.
- **Product launch:** customer-driven framing ("We heard you. / Departments asked for…"). Design-tradeoff naming. Specs line at the bottom.
- **Cart abandonment:** direct acknowledgment ("You left a kit in your cart"). Trust markers and completeness close the sale. Offer help. No discount.
- **Re-engagement:** most restrained send. Acknowledgment without guilt. "If not, we understand." Trust marker closes.
- **Departmental / B2B:** trade language freely. Civilian pairings optional. Still no hype.

## Dual-audience final check

Before hitting send, read the email twice:

1. **As an EMT:** Do the specifics (CAT tourniquet, HyFin, MOLLE, "trusted by 1,000+ departments") show up and hold up? Would you forward this to your lieutenant without cringing?
2. **As a concerned parent with no training:** Does the copy feel accessible? Are technical terms paired with plain-English categories? Does it build confidence rather than anxiety?

If either audience falls out at any point, revise.

---

## Reviewer handoff

Once this checklist passes, the send is ready for approval. Log any exceptions (dedicated B2B send, personal-voice send from named team member) with the reviewer so the deviation is traceable.
