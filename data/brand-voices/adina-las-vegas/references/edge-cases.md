# Adina Las Vegas — Edge Cases & Voice Boundaries

Companion file to `brand.md` and `copy-samples.md`. Read this before handling anything off-pattern: a brief that looks underspecified, a sensitive news moment, a non-Jewish holiday, an Armoire piece, a competitor comparison, or any send where the easy move would be the wrong one.

Three sections:
1. **Anti-paragons** — copy that's clearly *not* us, with diagnoses. Scan before writing to pre-empt the common failure modes.
2. **Edge cases & defaults** — how to handle sensitive topics, non-Jewish holidays, legal disclosures, Armoire, vague briefs, and the personal-letter vs. brand-voice call.
3. **Don't confuse our voice with…** — comparisons to competitors and adjacent aesthetics. Useful when a brief references another brand or when a draft starts drifting.

---

## 1. Anti-paragons — copy that's definitely NOT us

### Anti-paragon 1: Hype-y influencer launch

> *Subject: babe, 🔥 OUR NEWEST DROP IS HERE 🔥 you're GONNA FLIP*
>
> *Hey girlie!!! WE ARE SO OBSESSED with what's dropping today!!! 😍 You're going to LITERALLY flip — these are the prettiest pieces we've ever made!!! Run, don't walk — sizes are selling FAST. SHOP NOW before your size is GONE!!*

**Why this is wrong for us:**
- "Babe" / "girlie" is influencer-speak that our 30-something frum customer tunes out immediately.
- All-caps + emoji-stacking + multi-exclamation urgency triggers the spam-email reflex.
- "Obsessed" as a self-declaration is flat — customers can be obsessed in their own quote. We don't say *we* are.
- "Run, don't walk" + "FAST" + "GONE" is manufactured urgency. We trust the product to speak.
- Zero specificity — nothing about the actual pieces, fabric, fit, or occasion.

### Anti-paragon 2: Corporate / formal-placeholder voice

> *Subject: Exciting News from Adina Las Vegas*
>
> *Dear Valued Customer,*
>
> *We are pleased to announce the launch of our latest seasonal collection. Our team has worked diligently to curate a range of stunning, elevated pieces that we are confident will resonate with our discerning clientele. We invite you to explore our beautifully crafted garments at your earliest convenience.*
>
> *Warmest regards,*
> *The Adina Las Vegas Team*

**Why this is wrong for us:**
- "Dear Valued Customer" is the exact phrasing Michal explicitly forbids.
- "We are pleased to announce" is placeholder language with no voice — it could be from any brand.
- "Curate," "elevated," "stunning," "discerning clientele" — each is an overused word with no real meaning in context.
- "The Adina Las Vegas Team" on a launch send is cold. Michal is a real person. Use her, or use "Adina LV."
- Zero warmth. Zero product. Zero reason to care.

### Anti-paragon 3: Modesty-as-limitation framing

> *Subject: Finally — modest dresses that actually cover!*
>
> *Tired of dresses that show too much? Our new collection FINALLY gives you the coverage you've been waiting for. No more worrying about sleeves that are too short or necklines that are too low. Cover up in style!*

**Why this is wrong for us:**
- Frames modesty as relief from a problem rather than a way of being. Our customer doesn't think of modesty as a burden.
- "Cover up" is on our forbidden list — it treats modesty as hiding.
- Apologetic register ("Tired of..." / "FINALLY...") positions the brand as a solution to shame. Adina is a solution to *looking amazing,* not to embarrassment.
- Negative framing ("No more worrying") — we don't sell from fear.

## 2. Edge cases & defaults

### Sensitive topics (illness, death, major news, tragedy in Israel or the Jewish community)

- **Default:** don't reference directly in marketing sends.
- If a scheduled send conflicts with a real-world tragedy, the send is **delayed** — the copy is not "softened." Delay the email; don't write around it.
- If acknowledgment is required (e.g., an extended pause in sends), it comes in Michal's first-person voice, signed *— Michal,* and does not pivot to product.

### Holidays the brand doesn't officially recognize

- **Christmas:** acknowledge calendar-wise if needed ("year-end sale," "winter break") but never center it. No "Christmas collection."
- **Valentine's Day:** exception — we've sent "Treat Yourself" sends and those are fine as romantic-self-gifting, not as a Valentine's-specific holiday frame.
- **Easter:** don't reference.
- **Secular US holidays** (Memorial Day, Fourth of July, Labor Day): reference for sale timing if doing one, don't lean on the patriotic frame.

### Customer complaints or negative reviews

- **Default:** don't reference complaints in marketing.
- If addressing an ongoing issue (shipping delay, sizing confusion, site bug), Michal or the team writes a specific, plainspoken note in personal-letter mode. No "we hear you." Name the issue, name what's being done.

### Legal / compliance mentions

- **Final-sale notices:** always disclose in the body when an automatic discount applies.
- **Shipping thresholds:** *Free shipping on $150+.* State once per email where relevant.
- **Return policy:** not the lead of a marketing email. Can live in the footer or a secondary line.
- **Ad disclosures, terms, legal disclaimers:** 10pt gray at the bottom, never in the voice of the email.

### When the brief is vague

- Default to **restock-tone** (quiet, product-forward, low-energy) rather than launch-tone.
- If the discount structure isn't clear, ask one clarifying question before drafting — don't guess the percent or terms.
- If the audience isn't specified, default to the **primary customer** (see brand.md §2 Audience).

### When the product is outside our usual range (collabs, non-apparel)

- Stay on-brand. Don't shift voice to match the adjacent category.
- If we can't sell the item in our voice, we shouldn't be selling it — flag to the team instead of contorting the copy.

### Armoire Collection (other brands carried in our stores)

- We *carry* those brands; we don't *write as* them. Adina's voice frames the pieces: *"Pieces we love, from brands we trust. 40% off."*
- Don't over-promise on fit or fabric for pieces we didn't design.
- Don't highlight Armoire above our own line — it's a secondary offering.

### Personal-letter mode vs. brand-voice mode — which when?

- **Personal letter (Michal, first-person, signed *— Michal*):** Chag Sameach, Pesach letter, major milestones, end-of-year thank-you, apology for an outage, store opening announcement, founder Q&A.
- **Brand voice (we/our, signed *Adina LV* or nothing):** product launches, restocks, sales, editorial styling content, event invitations, most campaigns.
- **Default when in doubt:** brand voice. Personal-letter mode is intentional, not constant.

## 3. Don't confuse our voice with…

- **By Tess:** punchy-girly with trend-of-the-week energy (matcha, zodiac, "code-book" vibes). We're slower, warmer, more occasion-driven, and we don't chase micro-trends. Borrow their *clarity of POV,* not their volume or trend cycle.
- **Shop Dolly:** sells with confident over-promising. We sell with confident *specificity.* Same confidence, different technique.
- **Lunella, Avanel Couture, Udel:** direct competitors. We don't name them in copy. Our differentiator is fit + fabric + Michal's voice. We don't need to say what's wrong with theirs.
- **Frumpy / uniform-wear modest brands:** they sell modesty as the whole pitch. We sell design that happens to be modest.
- **Fast-fashion modest (Shein modest edits, low-price dupes):** they sell a look cheap. We sell a piece made well at a fair price. Never apologize for our prices or over-justify them — customers understand quality when the copy is calm.
- **Reformation / Aritzia:** aesthetically close in some ways (feminine cuts, considered silhouettes). They're secular; we're modest-first. Don't borrow their tone when they're winking at a late-20s non-observant shopper — our customer is in a different moment.
