# FatSwag

**Source:** migrated from `fatswag-brand-voice/SKILL.md` (v1)
**Status:** canonical
**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-19

## Essence

Inclusive streetwear brand (S–6X) built around Swag — founder, model, and voice. The brand announces; it doesn't sell. Closest voice reference: **GV Gallery**. Closest design references: **Vans, PacSun, Foot Locker.**

In Swag's own words: _"I created a space for people to come get fly. People who look just like me."_ That line is the brand. Copy should feel like it came from the person who said it — not from a team writing about him.

## Audience

18–35, streetwear culture, plus-size community, vocal and engaged. ~19K email subscribers, ~20K SMS (Postscript).

Two meaningful sub-audiences:
- **Beanie buyers** — speed and scarcity. Impatient. Short lead times.
- **Clothing buyers** — identity and belonging. Buy into the brand as much as the product.

Swag himself is inseparable from the brand — his personal and brand accounts run roughly 50/50. Subscribers don't distinguish between the man and the label.

## Voice

- **Is:** bold, direct, minimal, confident, community-driven
- **Is not:** corny, gimmicky, wordy, corporate, try-hard, preachy
- **Core tension:** _Serious but fun._ Swag calls it "busy" — takes the work seriously while still having fun doing it. Emails should feel like a brand that's moving, not one standing around trying to convince you.

**In a word: unapologetic.** The voice assumes you already know what FatSwag is. If you don't, you're about to find out.

## Writing Principles

1. **Less is more. Always.** Image-and-a-headline model. Design does the heavy lifting. Copy labels, announces, directs — never describes, persuades, or narrates. If a line could be two words, use two words.
2. **Announce, don't sell.** FatSwag emails sound like a brand posting a drop on Instagram, not a store sending a promo. "This just hit" — not "you should buy this because."
3. **Don't riff on the product.** If a shirt says "I used to be skinny, need money for a gym membership," the email does NOT say "get to the gym." Don't be clever with product text, don't extend the joke, don't play on the graphic. Show the piece and let it speak.
4. **Community first, product second.** "FOR THE PEOPLE" hits harder than "NEW T-SHIRT." The copy reminds them this brand exists for them — it doesn't pitch a transaction.
5. **Stay in the pocket.** Voice doesn't shift week to week. Same energy, same format, same cadence. One look and a subscriber should know it's FatSwag before reading a word.
6. **Never explain what FatSwag is.** If a line sounds like it belongs on an About page, it doesn't belong in an email. Swag doesn't position his brand — he runs it.

## Form & Format

- **Rhythm:** short, punchy, fragmented. No paragraphs in campaign emails. One line of supporting copy max beneath the headline — and even that's optional.
- **Capitalization:** ALL CAPS for headlines and hero text. Sentence case for subject lines and supporting copy. ALL CAPS in CTA buttons.
- **Length:** headline 2–5 words. Subject line 2–6 words. Body copy optional; if present, one line.
- **Emojis:** **never.** Not in subject lines, not in body, not in CTAs. This is a hard rule, not a preference — emojis break the minimal, product-forward aesthetic instantly.
- **First person:** brand voice by default — no "I." Swag's first-person is reserved for **milestone moments and welcome flow only**, and even then action-oriented ("Come get fly. New drops every week.") not storytelling ("I created this brand because...").
- **Punctuation quirks:** double question marks ("Wyd??") and double/triple exclamation ("!!!") carry over from SMS for conversational energy — used sparingly, and never in combination with fake urgency language.

## Subject Lines

- **Length:** 2–6 words. Short.
- **Tone:** insider. **Like a text from someone who knows you're paying attention.** Not trying to get your attention — assuming you already care. You're giving the signal, not making the pitch.
- **Good:**
  - "You already know."
  - "New drop. Friday."
  - "Wyd?? New drop just hit."
  - "Wake up. New beanies."
  - "Peep the new drop."
  - "This is not a drill."
  - "Don't sleep on these."
- **Bad:**
  - "Our Newest Collection Is Here!" — corporate, trying too hard.
  - "You Won't Want to Miss This Drop!" — bait cadence, not the voice.
  - "Check Out What Just Landed" — generic, no attitude.

## CTAs

- **Approved vocabulary:** drop-culture, direct, no filler. All caps in the button.
- **Good:**
  - SHOP THE DROP
  - COP YOURS
  - GET IT FIRST
  - SHOP NEW SZN
  - SHOP BEANIES
  - SHOP THE SALE
- **Bad:**
  - SHOP NOW — too generic
  - BUY NOW — too transactional
  - EXPLORE THE COLLECTION — too corporate
  - DON'T MISS OUT — too desperate

## Email Types

**Voice stays constant. Tone shifts with context.** Every email here is unmistakably FatSwag — same vocabulary, same restraint, same cadence. What moves is the posture the voice takes on for the moment: a drop announcement is hype; a post-purchase is gratitude; a welcome is a handshake from Swag. If the tone notes below aren't called out, assume default brand voice.

### Weekly Drop (primary)
- **Structure:** hero image + headline (2–5 words, all caps) + optional sub-line (1 sentence max) + CTA.
- **Copy approach:** announce the drop, name what it is, direct to the site. No product description. No feature list. No story.

### Beanie Drop
- **Structure:** same as weekly drop, often with colorway callout or "pre-order" tag.
- **Copy approach:** scarcity and speed over identity. Beanie buyers are impatient — they don't need to be sold, they need to be told.
- **Tone shift:** more urgent than a clothing drop. Short sub-lines ("Very limited. Tap in."), faster verbs.
- **Sample headlines:** "STRIPES ON TOP." / "THE CROWN IS BACK." / "BEANIES JUST HIT."

### Sale / Promo
- **Structure:** bold discount callout in the headline, time-boxed urgency in the sub-line, code if applicable, CTA.
- **Copy approach:** event-driven, not desperate. State the deal, state the window, done.
- **Tone shift:** the window is the story ("This weekend only." / "Next 60 minutes." / "48 hours.") — not the discount.

### VIP / Exclusive Access
- **Structure:** same frame, different framing. Headline signals exclusivity.
- **Copy approach:** email subscribers get something SMS doesn't. Lean into the "before everyone else" frame.
- **Tone shift:** insider over broadcast. A little quieter, a little more direct.
- **Sample headlines:** "VIP DROP." / "EMAIL ONLY." / "FIRST ACCESS." / "NOT FOR EVERYBODY."

### Product Feature
- **Structure:** spec-sheet fragments stacked under the headline, CTA below.
- **Copy approach:** 2–4 word callouts per line with cultural energy — not descriptions. Material. Fit feel. Shipping speed.
- **Good:** "Heavyweight. Soft. All day." / "Heavy fit. Ships tomorrow." / "Premium cotton. Won't crack. Won't fade."
- **Bad:** "This premium heavyweight fleece features a relaxed fit designed for comfort."

### Abandoned Cart (flow)
- **Structure:** 2-email sequence. Email 1 is reminder-only; Email 2 carries the discount.
- **Copy approach:**
  - Email 1 — no discount. "Don't sleep on it." + product image + FINISH YOUR ORDER
  - Email 2 — discount. "10% off to close it out." + code + COMPLETE YOUR ORDER
- **Tone shift:** slightly more conversational than a drop — Swag pulling you back in, not selling you something new.

### Welcome (flow)
- **Structure:** short welcome + "new drops every week" + Swag sign-off + CTA.
- **Copy approach:** action-oriented, not storytelling. "Come get fly. New drops every week."
- **Tone shift:** warmer than a drop — this is a handshake. First-person from Swag is allowed here (and essentially only here). Sign-off: `— Swag`.
- **Overrides:** the usual "no first-person" rule is lifted for this flow.

### Post-Purchase
- **Structure:** confirmation or shipping beat + short acknowledgement + tag-us nudge.
- **Copy approach:** short. Grateful. "Appreciate you." Follow-up: "Tag @itsfatswag when it hits."
- **Tone shift:** the customer just bought. This is gratitude and community, not a pitch. Don't upsell here.

## Banned Moves (Brand-Specific)

- **Brand positioning language.** "Real streetwear." / "Made for us." / "No compromise." / "Streetwear for every size." — About-page copy. Does not belong in emails.
- **"Real" as a qualifier.** "Real streetwear," "real fit," "real quality." Copywriter language. Swag doesn't validate his own brand.
- **Riffing on product graphics.** The shirt speaks for itself.
- **Over-explaining size inclusivity.** "Available in sizes S through 6X so everyone can..." — the brand IS inclusivity. Don't narrate it.
- **Invented slang.** Only use words Swag actually says. Check `references/sms-vocabulary.md` before using any slang term that isn't already confirmed.
- **Long subject lines.** "New drop. Friday." is all you need.
- **Paragraphs in campaign emails.** Image. Headline. CTA. Done.
- **Pity or sympathy framing.** "We know it's hard to find clothes that fit..." — confidence, not consolation.
- **Competitive positioning.** "Nobody else is doing this." Swag doesn't compare. He just drops.

_Universal banned moves (AI-ese, fake urgency, corporate cadence, generic e-com adjectives, pity framing, competitive trash-talk, etc.) apply automatically — see `references/universal-banned-moves.md`._

## Extras

- **Origin story (use sparingly, in welcome and milestone only).** FatSwag was born because Swag — plus-size himself — couldn't find clothes that looked right at his size. The brand's own words: _"I created a space for people to come get fly. People who look just like me."_ This is the truth underneath every drop. Don't narrate it in campaign emails; let it live in the welcome flow and occasional milestone sends where first-person from Swag is appropriate.
- **Email inherits SMS DNA with more room.** SMS voice (via Postscript) is the brand's DNA — hype-man energy, all-caps bursts, aggressive urgency. Email keeps the same vocabulary, attitude, brevity, no-emojis, and drop-culture framing. What email adds: occasional sub-lines, product feature callouts (2–4 word fragments with cultural energy), grid layouts, visual hierarchy. _Email is the album version; SMS is the single. Same artist, more space._
- **Body-positivity framing:** empower, never single out. No language around weight, dieting, or anything that frames plus-size as a problem.
- **History:** brand running over a decade, FatSwag name solidified about four years ago.
